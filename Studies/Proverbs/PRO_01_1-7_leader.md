# Leader's Notes — Proverbs 1:1-7 - The Purpose of Proverbs

## Verses

> **1** The proverbs of Solomon, son of David, king of Israel:
>
> **2** To know wisdom and instruction, to understand words of insight,
>
> **3** to receive instruction in wise dealing, in righteousness, justice, and equity;
>
> **4** to give prudence to the simple, knowledge and discretion to the youth—
>
> **5** Let the wise hear and increase in learning, and the one who understands obtain guidance,
>
> **6** to understand a proverb and a saying, the words of the wise and their riddles.
>
> **7** The fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge; fools despise wisdom and instruction.
>
> — *English Standard Version (ESV)*

---

## Overview

- **Setting**: Composed in or around Solomon's reign (c. 970–931 BC) at the peak of Israel's United Monarchy; Jerusalem is the cultural and administrative center.
- **Audience**: A *dual* audience uniquely named in the text — the **simple** (פְּתָאיִם, *peta'im*, unformed/open) and **youth** (נַעַר, *na'ar*) of verse 4, and the **wise** (חָכָם, *chakham*) and **understanding** (נָבוֹן, *navon*) of verse 5. The primary social setting is father-to-son household instruction (1:8); the secondary setting is Solomon's court and scribal training.
- **Author / Speaker**: Solomon, identified with maximum royal weight ("son of David, king of Israel"). His wisdom was a divine gift granted at Gibeon (1 Kings 3:4-15), not a human achievement. The book is composite (Solomon, "the Wise," Agur, Lemuel), but 1:1 governs the whole collection.
- **Immediate context**: 1:1-7 is the programmatic introduction to chapters 1-9 (the "ten instructions"), and ultimately to the entire book. Its motto recurs at 9:10, forming a massive inclusio around the Prologue section.
- **Genre / literary frame**: A wisdom-literature prologue containing three sub-genres: (a) superscription (v. 1, nominal sentence), (b) purpose-statement / catalogue (vv. 2-6, infinitive chain with a jussive interruption at v. 5), (c) thesis-motto (v. 7, antithetical parallelism). The closest ANE parallel is Egyptian *sebayt* (instruction) literature, but Proverbs alone grounds wisdom in the fear of YHWH rather than *ma'at* or fate.
- **Structural devices**: An inclusio of חָכְמָה וּמוּסָר (*chokmah umusar*, "wisdom and instruction") brackets vv. 2 and 7. A chiastic ring in vv. 2-6 frames two named audiences (simple/wise) inside universal goals. Seven verses for a complete unit; ~15 wisdom terms in 7 verses (semantic *copia*).
- **Big idea**: All genuine knowledge — every domain, every age — begins, rests, and is perfected in reverent submission to YHWH; to despise this wisdom is the definition of a fool.
- **Supporting movements**:
  - Verse 1: authority — divinely gifted royal wisdom is being offered.
  - Verses 2-4: purpose — wisdom is intellectual *and* ethical *and* practical, and it is offered first to the unformed.
  - Verse 5: wisdom is never finished business; the mature must keep listening.
  - Verses 6-7: wisdom requires hermeneutical skill (reading proverbs and riddles) and a moral foundation (the fear of the LORD); the only excluded reader is the willful fool.

## Info

### v.1
- **מִשְׁלֵי שְׁלֹמֹה בֶן־דָּוִד מֶלֶךְ יִשְׂרָאֵל** (*mishley Shelomoh ben-David melekh Yisra'el*) — verbless title; the fullest royal identification given any biblical author.
- **mashal** (H4912) means both *comparison* and (by a related root) *rule*; wordplay suggests the man who sees true comparisons is the one fit to rule.
- Solomon's wisdom was a divine gift at Gibeon (1 Kings 3:4-15; 4:29-34); the prologue claims that divine origin for what follows.
- **Shelomoh** resonates with **shalom**: the king of peace produces wisdom that yields *shalom*.
- Tragic irony: the wisest man failed to follow his own teaching (1 Kings 11:1-8); his life demonstrates that wisdom is daily discipline, not permanent possession.
- The superscription functions as a genre marker and a title for the entire composite book — analogous to Davidic attribution of the Psalter.

### v.2
- Opens an infinitive-construct chain (לָדַעַת, לְהָבִין...): vv. 2-6 are one cascading purpose statement governed by v. 1.
- **chokmah** (H2451, *wisdom*): not abstract knowledge but performative skill — craftsmanship, governance, moral discernment, the art of living well.
- **musar** (H4148, *instruction/discipline*): teaching *plus* correction; learning that involves pain, rebuke, and the shaping of character. LXX renders it *paideia*.
- **binah** (H998, *understanding*): from בין ("between") — the capacity to make distinctions, to separate truth from falsehood.
- The pair *chokmah / musar* will reappear in v. 7, forming an inclusio that closes the prologue.

### v.3
- **haskel** (Hiphil inf.) — "wise dealing," prudence that issues in *effective action*; bridges insight and conduct.
- Triad: **tsedeq** (H6664, righteousness — conformity to God's standard), **mishpat** (H4941, justice — right procedure and judgment), **mesharim** (H4339, equity — evenhandedness, impartiality).
- This triad is the language of royal and prophetic ethics (Psalm 99:4; Isaiah 11:4): wisdom's fruit is public justice, not private enlightenment.
- Wisdom is therefore *ethical* before it is clever; the book trains character, not merely intellect.

### v.4
- **peta'im** (פְּתָאיִם, plural of *peti*, H6612): the *simple* — not stupid, but unformed and *open*. Root פתה means "to be open"; the *peti* can be persuaded by either wisdom or folly.
- **na'ar** (נַעַר): the young, unformed person; overlaps with *peti* — the audience most in need of moral formation.
- **ormah** (H6195, *prudence/shrewdness*): same root as the serpent's "craftiness" in Genesis 3:1 — a morally ambivalent capacity now baptized under YHWH-fear.
- **mezimmah** (H4209, *discretion*): the ability to plan and weigh consequences — also morally neutral, here redirected.
- Note the democratization: ANE instruction literature targeted elites; Proverbs targets the vulnerable beginner.

### v.5
- The infinitive chain breaks: three jussive verbs (*let him hear, let him increase, let him acquire*) directly address the wise.
- **shama'** ("hear") is *Shema* language (Deut 6:4) — to hear is to obey.
- **leqach** (H3948, "learning"): from לקח ("to take") — learning as *taking in*; even the wise have more to receive.
- **tachbulot** (H8458, "guidance"): nautical imagery — rope-handling to steer a ship; life is a sea requiring skillful navigation, not a straight road.
- Pastoral force: wisdom is never finished. The moment one stops growing, the title "wise" expires.

### v.6
- Three literary forms now named: **mashal** (proverb/comparison), **melitsah** (figurative or satirical saying), **chidah** (riddle).
- Wisdom requires *hermeneutical* skill — the ability to read indirect, layered, allusive speech. Cf. Samson's *chidah* (Judges 14:14); the Queen of Sheba's testing *chidot* (1 Kings 10:1).
- Truth in Proverbs is not always stated plainly; some must be extracted by the trained mind. This addresses the *advanced* student.
- Catalogue function: vv. 2-6 are essentially a learning-objectives statement / table of contents for the book.

### v.7
- **יִרְאַת יְהוָה רֵאשִׁית דָּעַת** — verbless nominal sentence; the absence of a copula gives it the force of timeless ontological claim.
- **yir'at YHWH** (fear of the LORD) appears 14× in Proverbs — more than any other biblical book; it is the book's hermeneutical key.
- **reshit** (H7225, "beginning") carries three simultaneous senses: temporal *starting point*, qualitative *chief part*, logical *foundation*. The same word opens Genesis (בְּרֵאשִׁית) — cosmological and epistemological origins are linked.
- **da'at** (knowledge): relational/covenantal, not merely cognitive (cf. Genesis 4:1; Hosea 13:5).
- 7b is antithetical: **evilim** (H191, "fools") **bazu** ("despise") wisdom and *musar*. *Evil* is morally perverse — willful rejection, not ignorance.
- Inclusio: *chokmah umusar* in v. 2 returns in v. 7, sealing the prologue as a complete unit and dramatizing the choice: receive what is offered, or be defined by its rejection.

## Discussion

### Observation
1. The prologue names two audiences — the simple/youth (v. 4) and the wise/understanding (v. 5). What does this dual targeting tell us about how the writer views wisdom?
   - **Leader's note:** Wisdom is universally accessible *and* infinitely deep. It meets beginners with prudence and demands continued growth from the mature. No one outgrows it; no one is excluded from it (except by their own contempt). Surface the inclusivity of Hebrew wisdom over against Egyptian elite-only instruction.

2. List every word in vv. 2-6 that names a kind of wisdom or wise capacity. How many can you find?
   - **Leader's note:** About 15 distinct terms in six verses (wisdom, instruction, insight, wise dealing, righteousness, justice, equity, prudence, knowledge, discretion, learning, guidance, proverb, saying, riddles). This is rhetorical *copia*: wisdom is too large for any single word.

3. What two things does verse 7 contrast?
   - **Leader's note:** The fear of the LORD (positive — beginning of knowledge) vs. the fool's contempt (negative — despising wisdom and instruction). This antithetical parallelism introduces the "two ways" structure that pervades the whole book.

4. Where does the phrase "wisdom and instruction" appear in this passage? What effect does the repetition create?
   - **Leader's note:** Verse 2 and verse 7b — an inclusio that brackets the prologue. What v. 2 offers, v. 7 warns can be despised. The prologue is dramatized as an open door: enter or refuse.

### Interpretation
1. Why does the writer use "fear" rather than "love" or "respect" when describing the right disposition toward the LORD?
   - **Leader's note:** *Yir'ah* spans terror, awe, reverent submission, and covenantal obedience. It includes love (cf. Deut 6:13 alongside Deut 6:5) but refuses to soften God's holiness. Softer alternatives ("respect") lose the edge of standing before infinite holiness. The NT does not abolish this (Acts 9:31; 2 Cor 7:1; 1 Pet 1:17).

2. The Hebrew word *reshit* in v. 7 can mean *beginning*, *foundation*, or *chief part*. Which is correct — and what difference does it make?
   - **Leader's note:** All three simultaneously. If only "beginning," the fear of the LORD could be a starter that one outgrows; if "foundation" and "chief part" are included, it is the permanent ground of all knowing. Confirmed by 9:10 (same motto reused at the closing of the section) and Job 28:28.

3. What does it mean that fools "despise" wisdom? Is the fool's problem mental or moral?
   - **Leader's note:** The verb *bazah* is a verb of the *will*, not the intellect. The fool has the same access to wisdom as everyone else but holds it in contempt. The diagnosis is moral rebellion, not cognitive limitation. This is why folly cannot be cured by information alone.

4. How does the prologue relate to Genesis 3? Notice the words *ormah* (v. 4) and the desire to "be wise" (Gen 3:6, same root as *haskel* in v. 3).
   - **Leader's note:** Eden's tragedy was wisdom seized autonomously. Proverbs offers the corrective: the same capacities the serpent corrupted — shrewdness, knowledge, the desire to be wise — are reclaimed and placed under the fear of YHWH. Wisdom is *received*, not grasped.

### Application
1. Where in your daily life do you act as though knowledge is autonomous — as though you can think, decide, or work well without reference to God?
   - **Leader's note:** Push past "religious vs. secular" categories. The fear of the LORD applies in work, education, relationships, money, conversation. Read the "compartmentalization error" from the source's Application section. Concrete examples beat abstractions.

2. Verse 5 says even the wise must keep hearing and increasing. What does intentional ongoing growth look like for someone who has been a Christian for decades?
   - **Leader's note:** The complacency error — assuming this book is for beginners. Press toward: active pursuit of correction, sitting under teachers, joining groups where you are not the expert, reading challenging theology, accountability relationships. Solomon never stopped needing this; neither do we.

3. What does it look like, practically, to *receive correction* (musar) rather than despise it?
   - **Leader's note:** Source identifies the contempt patterns — defensiveness, mocking, echo chambers, dismissing rebuke as outdated. The wise person actively invites accountability; the fool insulates himself. Prov 9:8-9; 12:1; 15:31-32.

4. Verse 3 names *righteousness, justice, and equity* as the fruit wisdom produces. Where in your work, family, or community should those three qualities show up more clearly?
   - **Leader's note:** Wisdom is not private enlightenment but public justice. Apply concretely: business decisions, treatment of employees and the marginalized, fairness in family/church discipline, honesty in speech. Connects wisdom tradition to prophetic ethics (Micah 6:8).
