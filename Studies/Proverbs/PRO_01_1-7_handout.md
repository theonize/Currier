# Proverbs 1:1-7 - The Purpose of Proverbs — Study Handout

## Verses

> **1** The proverbs of Solomon, son of David, king of Israel:
>
> **2** To know wisdom and instruction, to understand words of insight,
>
> **3** to receive instruction in wise dealing, in righteousness, justice, and equity;
>
> **4** to give prudence to the simple, knowledge and discretion to the youth—
>
> **5** Let the wise hear and increase in learning, and the one who understands obtain guidance,
>
> **6** to understand a proverb and a saying, the words of the wise and their riddles.
>
> **7** The fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge; fools despise wisdom and instruction.
>
> — *English Standard Version (ESV)*

---

## Overview

Proverbs opens not with a proverb but with a prologue — seven verses that tell the reader what kind of book this is, who it is for, and how to read it. Solomon, identified at maximum royal weight, offers a collection of *meshalim* (proverbs/comparisons) whose purposes pile up across vv. 2-6 in a cascading chain of infinitives: to know, to understand, to receive, to give. Wisdom here is not abstract cleverness; it is intellectual, ethical, and practical formation that produces righteousness, justice, and equity in public life. The audience is double: the unformed beginner *and* the already-wise — because wisdom is never finished. Verse 7 then anchors everything in a single ontological claim: the fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge, and only the willful fool despises what is offered.

**Big idea:** All genuine knowledge — in every domain, in every age — begins, rests, and is perfected in the reverent fear of the LORD.

## Info

### v.1
- **mishley Shelomoh ben-David melekh Yisra'el** — the fullest royal byline given any biblical author; *mashal* means both "comparison" and (related root) "rule."
- Solomon's wisdom was a divine *gift* at Gibeon (1 Kings 3:4-15), not a personal achievement; the prologue claims that divine origin for the whole book.
- Tragic irony hangs over the title: the wisest man in Israel ultimately failed to follow his own teaching (1 Kings 11). Wisdom is a daily discipline, not a permanent possession.

### v.2
- Begins an infinitive chain governing vv. 2-6: the proverbs exist *for* something — purpose, not entertainment.
- **chokmah** (wisdom): performative skill, not theoretical knowledge — the art of living well.
- **musar** (instruction/discipline): teaching that includes correction and even pain. The LXX renders it *paideia*. Reappears in v. 7, bracketing the prologue.

### v.3
- **haskel** ("wise dealing"): insight that becomes effective action.
- Triad — **tsedeq** (righteousness), **mishpat** (justice), **mesharim** (equity) — the language of royal and prophetic ethics (Isa 11:4; Mic 6:8). Wisdom's fruit is public justice, not private enlightenment.

### v.4
- **peta'im** (simple): not stupid but *unformed* — the root *patah* means "to be open." Susceptible to either wisdom or folly. The book exists primarily for this person.
- **ormah** (prudence/shrewdness): same root as the serpent's "craftiness" in Genesis 3:1. Wisdom reclaims it and places it under YHWH-fear.
- The democratization is striking: ANE instruction texts targeted the sons of officials; Proverbs targets the most easily deceived.

### v.5
- The infinitive chain breaks and three jussives address the wise directly: *let him hear, let him increase, let him acquire.*
- **shama'** (hear) is *Shema* language — to hear in Hebrew is to obey.
- **tachbulot** (guidance): nautical imagery — handling ropes to steer a ship. Life is a sea, not a straight road; wisdom is the art of navigation.

### v.6
- Three literary genres named: **mashal** (proverb), **melitsah** (figurative saying), **chidah** (riddle, cf. Samson; Queen of Sheba).
- Wisdom requires *hermeneutical* skill: not all truth is on the surface. The discerning mind must penetrate indirect, layered speech.

### v.7
- The book's motto. The same construction recurs at 9:10, sealing chapters 1-9 in an inclusio.
- **yir'at YHWH** — comprehensive disposition: awe, reverent submission, covenantal trust, moral restraint. Stronger than "respect," not reducible to "terror."
- **reshit** (beginning) holds three senses at once — *starting point*, *foundation*, and *chief part*. The fear of the LORD is not a rung to climb past but the ground that supports the whole house.
- The word echoes Genesis 1:1 (בְּרֵאשִׁית): God's creative act is the *reshit* of the cosmos; the fear of the LORD is the *reshit* of knowledge.
- 7b — **fools despise** (*bazu*) wisdom and instruction. The verb is *volitional*. The fool is not ignorant; he refuses. The diagnosis is moral, not intellectual.

## Discussion

1. The prologue names two audiences — the simple/youth (v. 4) and the wise/understanding (v. 5). What does it tell us about wisdom that *both* are addressed?

2. Count the distinct words for wisdom-related qualities in vv. 2-6. Why pile up so many? What is the writer trying to communicate by accumulation?

3. The Hebrew *reshit* in v. 7 can mean *beginning*, *foundation*, or *chief part*. Which reading shapes your life most — and where does the difference show up?

4. Verse 7b says fools *despise* wisdom. Is foolishness, biblically, a mental problem or a moral one? What follows from your answer?

5. Where in your daily life do you act as though knowledge is autonomous — as though you could think, decide, or work well apart from the fear of the LORD?

6. Verse 5 commands the already-wise to keep hearing and increasing. What does *ongoing* growth look like for a Christian who has known the Bible for decades?

7. Wisdom's fruit, per v. 3, is *righteousness, justice, and equity* — public realities, not private feelings. Where in your work, family, or community do those three need to show up more clearly?
