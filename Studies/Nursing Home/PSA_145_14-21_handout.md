# Exegetical Analysis of Psalms 145:14-21 - The Lord upholds all who fall — Study Handout

## Verses

> **14** The LORD upholds all who are falling and raises up all who are bowed down.
>
> **15** The eyes of all look to you, and you give them their food in due season.
>
> **16** You open your hand; you satisfy the desire of every living thing.
>
> **17** The LORD is righteous in all his ways and kind in all his works.
>
> **18** The LORD is near to all who call on him, to all who call on him in truth.
>
> **19** He fulfills the desire of those who fear him; he also hears their cry and saves them.
>
> **20** The LORD preserves all who love him, but all the wicked he will destroy.
>
> **21** My mouth will speak the praise of the LORD, and let all flesh bless his holy name forever and ever.
>
> -- *English Standard Version (ESV)*

---

## Overview

The closing eight verses of David's acrostic (Hebrew letters samek through tav) shift register from royal trumpet to shepherd's cadence: YHWH upholds, feeds, hears, saves, preserves. A string of active participles — **סוֹמֵךְ** (*somek*, "upholds"), **פּוֹתֵחַ** (*potech*, "opens"), **שׁוֹמֵר** (*shomer*, "preserves") — paints providence as God's *continuous* mode of being, not occasional intervention. The word "all" (*kol*) recurs about eleven times, sweeping the vision outward to "every living thing" and "all flesh." A bodily spine runs through the section — eyes that look (v.15), a hand that opens (v.16), a mouth that praises (v.21) — tracing the cycle of dependence and response. At its heart, v.17 ("righteous and kind") echoes the mercy-credo of vv.8-9, grounding universal provision in revealed character rather than sentiment.

**Big idea:** YHWH is the universal, ever-active Provider whose righteous-and-kind character upholds the helpless, answers honest prayer, and draws all flesh into endless praise.

## Info

### v.14
- **סוֹמֵךְ / זוֹקֵף** (*somek / zoqef*) — participles of continuous upholding and lifting; *samak* = to take weight.
- "Bowed down" names the structurally vulnerable in a society with no welfare net.

### vv.15-16
- "Eyes of all look" — idiom for hopeful waiting (cf. Ps 121:1); "food in due season" confesses YHWH's rule over the harvest calendar.
- "You open your hand" — ANE provision motif democratized to "every living thing"; heart of the Jewish grace-after-meals.

### v.17
- **צַדִּיק** (*tsaddiq*, "righteous") + **חָסִיד** (*chasid*, "kind") — YHWH is himself kind; the character-credo paralleling vv.8-9.

### v.18
- Sound-play **קָרוֹב / קֹרְאָיו** (*qarov / qor'ayw*, "near / callers"); "in truth" (*be'emet*) — sincerity, not pedigree, is the qualifier.

### vv.19-20
- Cascade: fulfills, hears, saves. Sound-play **שׁוֹמֵר / יַשְׁמִיד** (*shomer / yashmid*, "preserves / destroys") — one character holding mercy and justice. Two-ways binary (Ps 1).

### v.21
- Inclusio with v.1; "all flesh" (*kol-basar*) widens the lone worshiper's vow into a cosmic chorus, bridging to Pss 146-150.

## Discussion

1. List the verbs whose subject is YHWH in vv.14-20. What do the participles ("upholds," "opens," "preserves") tell us about how God acts?
2. Count the times "all" appears. What is the effect of the repetition, and who is included?
3. Follow the body parts — eyes (v.15), hand (v.16), mouth (v.21). What cycle do they trace between creature and Creator?
4. Why does v.17 pair "righteous in all his ways" with "kind in all his works"? What goes wrong if we keep only one?
5. What does "in truth" (v.18) add — and how do we tell sincere prayer from going through the motions?
6. v.20 says God "preserves all who love him" but "all the wicked he will destroy." Why might we be tempted to drop the second half, and why does the psalm keep it?
7. When your needs are met, do you credit your own resources or YHWH's open hand (v.16)? How might you become a junior partner in that open hand toward others?
