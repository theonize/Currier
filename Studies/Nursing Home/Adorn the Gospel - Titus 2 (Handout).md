# Adorn the Gospel — A Group Study on Titus 2:1-10

> But as for you, teach what accords with sound doctrine.
> Older men are to be sober-minded, dignified, self-controlled, sound in faith, in love, and in steadfastness.
> Older women likewise are to be reverent in behavior, not slanderers or slaves to much wine. They are to teach what is good,
> and so train the young women to love their husbands and children,
> to be self-controlled, pure, working at home, kind, and submissive to their own husbands, that the word of God may not be reviled.
> Likewise, urge the younger men to be self-controlled.
> Show yourself in all respects to be a model of good works, and in your teaching show integrity, dignity,
> and sound speech that cannot be condemned, so that an opponent may be put to shame, having nothing evil to say about us.
> Slaves are to be submissive to their own masters in everything; they are to be well-pleasing, not argumentative,
> not pilfering, but showing all good faith, so that in everything they may adorn the doctrine of God our Savior.
> *(Titus 2:1-10, ESV)*

*A note about Paul and Titus:* Paul wrote this letter in his late ministry, between his two Roman imprisonments.  He had stopped on the island of Crete with his younger colleague Titus, helped plant small churches in several towns, and sailed on — leaving Titus to "put what remained into order."  Crete was a difficult place by everyone's reckoning, even the Cretans' own poets.  This is a letter for a small, new, mixed-up church on a rough island.  And notice who Paul addresses **first**: not the leaders, not the new converts — the *older men* and the *older women*.  They go to the front of the line.  This is a letter for people who have been around a long time.

---

## 1. Sound Doctrine Looks Like Something (v.1)

Paul opens by giving Titus his job: *teach what accords with sound doctrine*.

The word "sound" here is the Greek **hygiainō** — the root of our English word *hygiene*.  Healthy doctrine.  The picture is medical: true teaching produces healthy lives; sick teaching produces sick souls.

- Sound doctrine is not abstract — it shows up in how a person lives
- A long life of trust has a *shape* — patience, kindness, steadiness, hope
- The proof of what you believe is not what you can recite.  It is what is left in your bones when most of the recitation is gone
- This is good news for this room: you may not be able to argue theology anymore.  You can still **embody** it
- A doctrine that has shaped your character is *more* real now than it was when you could explain it

---

## 2. Older Saints Set the Tone (vv.2-3)

Paul addresses older men first, older women second, younger women and men after.  The gray-haired sit nearest the front of his moral line.

For **older men**, five marks: sober-minded, dignified, self-controlled, sound in faith, in love, and in **steadfastness** (*hypomonē* — the active staying-put under load that only long obedience can produce).

For **older women**, three things and a job:
- Reverent in behavior — literally, *fit for sacred things* (*hieroprepēs*).  Temple-language applied to ordinary daily bearing
- Not slanderers — the Greek is *diabolos*, the same word elsewhere translated **"devil."**  Paul calls gossip what it is
- Not slaves to wine
- **Teachers of good** (*kalodidaskaloi*) — older women are given a teaching ministry by Paul's own pen

A few things to notice:

- *Hypomonē* is the older man's signature virtue.  Stay put under load.  Do not drift in the last mile
- "Fit for sacred things" — your daily walk has sacred weight, even when no one is watching
- Watch the speech.  A nursing home — like every place humans gather — runs on talk.  Refuse the devilish kind, even when it is dressed up as concern
- Watch the cup.  Substances enslave at any age
- You are a teacher whether you ever stand in front of anyone again — by bearing, by story, by prayer

---

## 3. Pass It On (vv.4-6)

Note who Paul does *not* send to teach the young wives: he does not send Titus.  He sends the **older women**.

Paul knows what every elderly saint also knows — peer-elder teaching is more durable than instruction from an outside expert.  The home is the first seminary.  Older women train younger women in marriage, motherhood, household management, faith.

He uses one cardinal virtue — *sōphrōn*, self-control — **four times in six verses**.  It binds every age and gender together.  Older men, older women, younger women, younger men — all need this single virtue.

- The chain depends on the older generation taking the initiative
- You may not see the fruit.  You don't need to.  You are not the harvester
- One younger person, one piece of wisdom, one prayer, one story — that is real training
- For older men: think of one younger man you can show what *sōphrōn* costs in the real world
- For older women: think of one younger woman who could hear from you what you have learned about love, patience, prayer, and faithfulness
- The seeds you plant now may not sprout for twenty years.  Plant them anyway

---

## 4. Adorn the Doctrine (vv.7-10)

Paul pivots to Titus himself, then to the slaves of the household — and lands the chapter on a stunning sentence (v.10):

> *"that they may adorn the doctrine of God our Savior."*

**Adorn** translates the Greek word *kosmeō* — the root of our English word *cosmetic*.  Make beautiful.  Decorate.

And the one Paul gives this assignment to is **the slave** — the most legally diminished person in the entire household.  The gospel does not need your platform.  It needs your faithfulness.  The lowest-status person in Paul's world is the one whose daily life *beautifies* the highest reality (the doctrine of God our Savior).

The principle carries:

- Titus is to be a *typos* — a stamp, a model, a pattern.  His life is the impression that imprints others
- "Sound speech that cannot be condemned" — words critics cannot legitimately attack
- The slave's "weapons" are not big: not argumentative, not pilfering, showing good faith.  Small, hidden, daily
- Twice Paul names the public purpose of all this: *"that the word of God may not be reviled"* (v.5) and *"that they may adorn the doctrine"* (v.10).  Christian living is missionary

**For us:**

- The most diminished person in this room can adorn the gospel.  This is Paul's claim, not mine
- This is not about platforms — it is about daily faithfulness that makes Jesus look like he is worth following
- Concretely: a thank-you to the aide; refusing to badmouth the staff; not pilfering small advantages, attention, or extra portions; receiving care with grace; greeting the next person in the hallway by name; speaking sound, healthy words instead of complaint
- Your faithful smallness in this season may be the only sermon some watching person ever sees
- The gospel does not need your strength.  It needs your faithfulness.  *Kosmeō* is well within reach

## Review & Reflection

1. "Sound doctrine" (v.1) is *healthy* doctrine.  If a stranger watched you this week, what kind of doctrine would they say is "healthy" in your life?

2. Paul lists five marks of a faithful older man — including *steadfastness* (*hypomonē*, staying put under load).  Where in your life today is steadfastness still being asked of you?

3. Paul calls slander "devilish" (*diabolos* is literally "devil").  Where, if anywhere, does slander show up in your speech — even slander dressed up as concern?

4. Older women are *teachers of good* (*kalodidaskaloi*).  Who is one younger person you are positioned to teach this week — by story, prayer, or example?

5. Verse 10 says even slaves *adorn* the doctrine.  What is one small, hidden act in your day that adorns the gospel right where you are?

6. Twice Paul names the witness purpose: *"that the word of God may not be reviled... that they may adorn the doctrine."*  When was the last time you weighed something you said by that scale?

7. If everything else were stripped away — strength, memory, position — would you still believe what Paul calls *sound doctrine*?  What part of it would be left?

### For This Week

- **One small adornment.** Choose one daily act — a thank-you to an aide, a smile across the dining room, refusing one piece of gossip, a kind word to a roommate — and do it every day this week.  *Kosmeō.* Make the gospel beautiful in your one small corner

- **One Lois move.** Pick one younger person — a grandchild, niece, nephew, nurse, visitor — and tell them one thing the Lord has taught you through a long obedience.  A card, a phone call, a sentence at dinner.  You are a *kalodidaskalos* (teacher of good) by Paul's own word

The Lord who has been training you this long is still training you.  The doctrine of God our Savior is still beautiful — and you are still positioned to adorn it.
