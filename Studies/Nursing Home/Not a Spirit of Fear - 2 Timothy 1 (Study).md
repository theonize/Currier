# Not a Spirit of Fear

## A Group Study on 2 Timothy 1:1-14

> Paul, an apostle of Christ Jesus by the will of God according to the promise of the life that is in Christ Jesus,
> To Timothy, my beloved child: Grace, mercy, and peace from God the Father and Christ Jesus our Lord.
> I thank God whom I serve, as did my ancestors, with a clear conscience, as I remember you constantly in my prayers night and day.
> As I remember your tears, I long to see you, that I may be filled with joy.
> I am reminded of your sincere faith, a faith that dwelt first in your grandmother Lois and your mother Eunice and now, I am sure, dwells in you as well.
> For this reason I remind you to fan into flame the gift of God, which is in you through the laying on of my hands,
> for God gave us a spirit not of fear but of power and love and self-control.
> Therefore do not be ashamed of the testimony about our Lord, nor of me his prisoner, but share in suffering for the gospel by the power of God,
> who saved us and called us to a holy calling, not because of our works but because of his own purpose and grace, which he gave us in Christ Jesus before the ages began,
> and which now has been manifested through the appearing of our Savior Christ Jesus, who abolished death and brought life and immortality to light through the gospel,
> for which I was appointed a preacher and apostle and teacher,
> which is why I suffer as I do. But I am not ashamed, for I know whom I have believed, and I am convinced that he is able to guard until that Day what has been entrusted to me.
> Follow the pattern of the sound words that you have heard from me, in the faith and love that are in Christ Jesus.
> By the Holy Spirit who dwells within us, guard the good deposit entrusted to you.
> *(2 Timothy 1:1-14, ESV)*

---

### Setting the Scene

When Paul wrote this letter, he was in a Roman dungeon — not the comfortable house arrest of his earlier imprisonment, but a cold stone cell beneath the Forum.
He was old.
He was cold (he asks for his cloak in chapter 4).
Most of his friends had left him.
He knew his execution was coming soon — and it did, within months.

From that confinement, knowing his days were short, he wrote one last letter to a younger pastor he loved like a son.
This is not theology in the abstract.
This is a father writing his will.
Every word is weighted.

The themes Paul lands on are the themes of an old saint at the end of his road: *remember*, *don't be afraid*, *don't be ashamed*, *Christ has beaten death*, *hand it on*.

That is a letter for this room.

## 1. Faith That Was Passed Down (vv.3-5)

**Focus:** *"I am reminded of your sincere faith, a faith that dwelt first in your grandmother Lois and your mother Eunice and now, I am sure, dwells in you as well."* (v.5)

### Context / Setup
- Paul writes from a Roman prison; Timothy is pastoring in Ephesus, about 800 miles away
- Timothy was probably in his mid-thirties — Paul's "beloved child" in the faith
- His mother Eunice was a believing Jewish woman; his father was a Greek who is conspicuously absent from the story (Acts 16:1)
- His grandmother **Lois** appears nowhere else in the Bible — but Paul names her.  She mattered
- Three generations of faith: Lois → Eunice → Timothy.  Two women raise a pastor
- Paul prays for Timothy "night and day" — the man in chains is praying for the man on the front lines

### Theological Anchor
- Faith is **inherited**, but every generation must own it personally
- "Sincere" (Greek: *anypokritos*) — literally "un-hypocritical," without a stage mask.  Real faith, not performance
- Tears are not weakness — they are evidence of love.  Paul cried, Timothy cried, and that is good
- Memory before God is one of the oldest spiritual disciplines: "I remember you constantly in my prayers"
- The home is the first seminary

### Language Notes
- **Lois** (Greek: Λωΐς) — an obscure name, possibly meaning "agreeable" or "better"; we know nothing else about her except that her grandson became a pastor
- **Eunice** (Greek: Εὐνίκη, *Eunike*) — "good victory"
- **Timothy** (Greek: Τιμόθεος, *Timotheos*) — "one who honors God"
- **Remember** (Greek: *anamimnēskō*) — to call back to mind on purpose, not a passing thought

### Talking Points
- Paul has every reason to be self-focused.  He is the one in chains.  Yet his opening prayer is for someone else
- Note what Paul thanks God for: not for Timothy's accomplishments, but for the **faith** in him
- The faith chain skips the father here.  God can build a faith line through whichever generation is willing
- Paul did not start Timothy's faith — he received Timothy as already loved, already taught, already prayed over by women he never met
- Pauline ministry stands on the shoulders of unnamed grandmothers

### For Us
- Many in this room are the Lois or Eunice in someone's life — even if you don't see the results
- A child can grow up, drift away, look like the faith didn't take — and then, twenty years later, it surfaces.  The seeds you planted are still in the ground
- You may not know who is praying for you, either.  Someone, somewhere, prayed you into this room
- Prayers across distance still count.  Paul prayed across 800 miles; you can pray across the country
- Stories matter.  The grandchild who asks "tell me again about when you were young" is doing real theology
- A handwritten letter, a phone call where you say "I'm praying for you by name" — these are Lois moves
- You don't need a stage or a soapbox.  Lois never had one
- If your faith feels small or worn — remember that it was poured into you by people whose names you might not even know

## 2. Fan the Flame (vv.6-7)

**Focus:** *"Fan into flame the gift of God, which is in you... for God gave us a spirit not of fear but of power and love and self-control."* (vv.6-7)

### Context / Setup
- At some point earlier, Paul (with the elders, 1 Timothy 4:14) had laid hands on Timothy and prayed over him for ministry
- Timothy seems to have been timid by nature — Paul has to keep encouraging him to stand firm (1 Cor 16:10; 1 Tim 4:12; 5:23)
- Paul does **not** tell Timothy to light a new fire.  The fire is already burning.  It has fuel.  It needs air (pneuma)
- The Greek picture is of glowing embers: lean in, blow gently, and the flame jumps back up
- This is written to someone in his thirties — but the principle holds for someone in their nineties

### Theological Anchor
- The Spirit's gift is given once and is real, but it is not on autopilot — it must be **stirred**
- The "spirit of fear" Paul names is **cowardice** (Greek: *deilia*) — shrinking back, not reverent caution
- The Spirit produces three things in v.7:
  - **Power** (*dynamis*) — God-given strength, not human grit
  - **Love** (*agapē*) — settled, sacrificial care for others
  - **Self-control** (*sōphronismos*) — a sound, ordered mind; not panicked, not chaotic
- These are gifts, not personality types — they can be received at any age

### Language Notes
- **Fan into flame** (Greek: *anazōpyreō*) — built from *ana-* (up, again) + *zō-* (life) + *pyr* (fire). Literally, "fire back to life." A blacksmith's word — you blow on the coals
- **Spirit of fear** (Greek: *pneuma deilias*) — cowardice, timidity.  Not the reverent "fear of the Lord" — this is the cringe of shrinking back
- **Self-control** (Greek: *sōphronismos*) — a sound mind, a disciplined mind; one of the four classical virtues, reframed as a gift of the Spirit

### Talking Points
- Notice the **passive** language: God gave us a Spirit.  Paul does not say "summon your courage."  He says: stir what the Spirit has already deposited
- Embers can sit under ash for a long time and still relight.  Cold faith is not dead faith — it is unstirred faith
- "Fan" is gentle.  You do not whack embers with a sledgehammer; you breathe on them
- A timid pastor with the Holy Spirit is more dangerous to the kingdom of darkness than a confident pastor without him
- The cure for fear is not bravery — it is remembering what the Spirit gave you;  remembering who you are...where you are...and who is with you

### For Us
- Many people say, "I used to teach Sunday School / lead a Bible study / sing in choir / volunteer — but I can't anymore."  Hear Paul: God did not take the gift back when your body slowed down
- The form changes; the gift remains.  A retired teacher still teaches — one-on-one with the aide.  A retired pastor still pastors — by listening to the resident next door
- **Fan-the-flame practices for any age:**
  - Pray for one person every morning, out loud, by name
  - Tell one story of God's faithfulness to one person this week
  - Open the Bible at the same time every day, even for ten minutes
  - Notice when someone is hurting and ask them how they are *really* doing
- These are small breaths on the embers: yours and theirs.  Do them, and the fire jumps
- Fear of being a burden, fear of being forgotten, fear of dying can dominate.  Paul says: that spirit is not from God

## 3. Not Ashamed (vv.8-12)

**Focus:** *"Therefore do not be ashamed of the testimony about our Lord, nor of me his prisoner... I am not ashamed, for I know whom I have believed."* (vv.8, 12)

### Context / Setup
- The Roman world ran on **honor and shame**.  To be publicly associated with a state criminal — even one you loved — was social ruin
- After Nero blamed Christians for the great fire of Rome (AD 64), simply being known as a Christian could cost you property, family, or your life
- "Me his prisoner" is striking.  Paul refuses to identify himself as Nero's prisoner.  He is Christ's prisoner — chained, yes, but by a different Master
- Three times the "ashamed" word group appears in this chapter (vv.8, 12, 16) — Paul is hammering it on purpose

### Theological Anchor
- The cross was a shame instrument before it was a salvation symbol.  To follow a crucified Lord was, by Roman standards, a humiliation
- The gospel re-locates honor.  The world's verdict does not get the last word — God's "that Day" does (v.12)
- v.10 contains the **pastoral heart of the whole passage** — Christ Jesus *"abolished death and brought life and immortality to light"*
- "Abolished" (Greek: *katargeō*) does not mean death no longer happens — it means death has been **defanged**.  The lion is on a leash.  The sting is gone
- Christian assurance is not a personality trait.  It is **knowing whom you have believed** (v.12)

### Language Notes
- **Ashamed** (Greek: *epaischynomai*) — an intensified form, "deeply ashamed, embarrassed before others"
- **Share in suffering** (Greek: *synkakopatheō*) — a Pauline coinage from *syn* (with) + *kakos* (bad) + *paschō* (suffer).  "Co-suffer."  A word Paul uses only in 2 Timothy
- **Abolished** (Greek: *katargeō*) — to render inoperative, decommission, take out of service.  Same word Paul uses for Christ's defeat of the powers
- **Brought to light** (Greek: *phōtizō*) — to illuminate.  Christ turned the spotlight on a hidden room: there is life after death, and it is unending
- **That Day** (Greek: *ekeinē hē hēmera*) — the Day of the Lord; the final reckoning when every faithful witness is vindicated

### Talking Points
- Paul writes "I am not ashamed" from a cold cell, abandoned, hours from execution.  The world's evidence said he had been defeated.  He simply did not believe it
- "I know whom I have believed" — not "I know **what** I have believed."  It is a Person, not a position
- Paul does not say *"I won't be ashamed"* (future) or *"I try not to be ashamed"* (effort).  He says: I am not ashamed. **Settled.**
- The combination is striking — *suffer* + *not ashamed* + *Christ abolished death*.  Suffering does not get the last word, because death does not get the last word
- v.10 is one of the most quietly explosive lines in the New Testament.  Christ has already done it.  The greatest enemy is already beaten

### For Us
- Many in this room have walked beside people who died well — and beside people who died afraid.  Paul speaks to both
- **The fear of death** is the largest unspoken weight in a place like this.  v.10 lifts it
- You may be tempted to be ashamed of your present state — needing help, not remembering as well, feeling forgotten, having visible weakness.  Hear Paul, chained in a dungeon: *"I am not ashamed"*
- Honor-relocation:
  - The world honors usefulness — God honors faithfulness
  - The world honors strength — God honors trust
  - The world honors independence — God honors love
  - The world fears death — Christ has abolished it
- Things Christ has *already* done (notice the past tense in v.9-10):
  - Saved us
  - Called us to a holy calling
  - Given us grace in Christ before the ages began
  - Manifested himself in the appearing of Christ
  - **Abolished death**
  - Brought life and immortality to light
- If you have believed in him, none of this is in question.  The work is done

## 4. Guard the Deposit (vv.13-14)

**Focus:** *"Follow the pattern of the sound words that you have heard from me... By the Holy Spirit who dwells within us, guard the good deposit entrusted to you."* (vv.13-14)

### Context / Setup
- "Deposit" (Greek: *parathēkē*) was a legal and commercial word — a valuable item placed in a friend's keeping for safekeeping, with a sacred duty to return it intact
- In Paul's world, breaking a *parathēkē* was a serious crime — like a banker robbing his own customer's safe deposit box
- Paul is dying.  The gospel he received from Christ, taught for thirty years, and now writes down — Paul is handing it over to Timothy
- "Sound words" (*hygiainontōn logōn*) — literally **healthy** words.  Words that produce health in the soul.  The opposite is sick teaching, which produces sick souls

### Theological Anchor
- The gospel has **content**.  It is not a vibe, a mood, or a private spirituality.  It is news with shape and substance
- Faithful ministry is not invention — it is **stewardship**
- We do not generate the gospel; we receive it, guard it, and pass it on
- We do not guard it alone — the Holy Spirit who dwells within us is the chief guardian (v.14)
- The pattern goes: God deposits the gospel with Paul → Paul deposits it with Timothy → Timothy with "faithful men" (2 Tim 2:2) → and on down to us

### Language Notes
- **Deposit / entrusted** (Greek: *parathēkē*) — that which is placed alongside; a sacred trust
- **Guard** (Greek: *phylassō*) — actively keep watch over; the word for a sentry or a shepherd on the night watch
- **Pattern** (Greek: *hypotypōsis*) — an outline, a model, a stamp.  Not a verbatim formula, but a recognizable shape
- **Sound** (Greek: *hygiainō*) — the root of our English word *hygiene*.  Healthy, life-giving doctrine

### Talking Points
- Paul does not say "find your own truth."  He says, "follow the pattern of the sound words you heard from me"
- A deposit can be lost, watered down, edited, or stolen.  Each generation has to guard it again
- "By the Holy Spirit who dwells within us" — the work of guarding is not yours alone.  The same Spirit who indwells you indwells the next guardian
- Notice "good deposit."  Not "burdensome deposit."  Not "outdated deposit."  The gospel is **good news**, and guarding it is a privilege
- The chain stretches both backwards and forwards.  Someone handed it to you.  You hand it on to someone else

### For Us
- You are a link in a chain that began with Christ and runs through Lois, Eunice, Timothy, the church fathers, the Reformers, the missionaries, the parents and pastors who taught you — and runs forward through you to the next generation
- **What you can deposit, even from this room:**
  - The truth that Jesus saves
  - The story of how he saved you in particular
  - The promise that he will keep what you've entrusted to him
  - The hope of resurrection
  - The pattern of sound prayer, sound speech, sound character
- Practical handoffs:
  - Write down the story of how you came to faith — even one page — for grandchildren or great-grandchildren
  - Tell one young person in this facility (a nurse, an aide, a visitor) one true thing about Jesus this week
  - Make sure your family knows what you want said at your funeral — what gospel you want preached
  - Pray that the next generation in your line knows the Lord
- The Spirit who indwells you is doing the deepest guarding work, even when you sleep

## Review & Reflection

These questions draw from all four sections. Pick the ones that fit your group; you will not get to them all. Leave silences. Some of the best answers come slowly.

1. When you hear the word "fear," what is the first fear that comes to your mind right now? How would it change if you heard Paul whisper to you, "God did not give you that spirit"?

2. Who is the Lois or the Eunice in your story? Who first taught you about Jesus? Take a moment and say their name out loud.

3. Whose grandmother are you — even informally? Whose Lois? Who is watching how you live and remembering what you say about the Lord?

4. Paul says God gave us a Spirit of **power, love, and self-control**. Which of those three feels most absent in your life right now? Which feels most present?

5. Paul wrote, "I am not ashamed, for I know whom I have believed." Notice it is *whom*, not *what*. How would you say it in your own words? "I am not ashamed because I know ____."

6. Verse 10 says Christ "abolished death and brought life and immortality to light." If you really believed that this morning — fully, plainly — what would change about today?

7. What "gift" or calling has felt cold or far away lately? What is one small breath you could give to the embers this week?

8. The world tells us older saints have less to offer. Paul writes the opposite from a prison cell. What is something — a prayer, a story, a piece of wisdom, a kindness — that only **you** are positioned to deposit in someone this week?

9. If you wrote a one-page "deposit" for your grandchildren or great-grandchildren — the most important things you have learned about Jesus — what would be on it?

10. What is one verse from this passage you would like to memorize, or hold onto, or hear again before you leave this room today?

### This Week — Two Practices

- **Fan one ember.** Pick one spiritual practice that has gone cold — daily prayer, reading a Psalm, singing a hymn, naming one person before God — and do it once a day this week. Small breaths. The flame returns
- **Make one deposit.** Choose one person — a grandchild, a nurse, a fellow resident, an old friend — and tell them one true thing about the Lord this week. A short note, a prayer over the phone, a sentence at the meal table. You are a guardian of a good deposit. Hand a little of it on
