# Loved into Splendor

## A Group Study on Ephesians 5:22-33

> Wives, submit to your own husbands, as to the Lord.
> For the husband is the head of the wife even as Christ is the head of the church, his body, and is himself its Savior.
> Now as the church submits to Christ, so also wives should submit in everything to their husbands.
>
> Husbands, love your wives, as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her,
> that he might sanctify her, having cleansed her by the washing of water with the word,
> so that he might present the church to himself in splendor, without spot or wrinkle or any such thing, that she might be holy and without blemish.
> In the same way husbands should love their wives as their own bodies. He who loves his wife loves himself.
> For no one ever hated his own flesh, but nourishes and cherishes it, just as Christ does the church,
> because we are members of his body.
> "Therefore a man shall leave his father and mother and hold fast to his wife, and the two shall become one flesh."
> This mystery is profound, and I am saying that it refers to Christ and the church.
> However, let each one of you love his wife as himself, and let the wife see that she respects her husband.

*A note about Paul:* When he wrote this letter, he was under house arrest in Rome — a shut-in, chained to a guard, confined to a small room. Paul himself was single — whether he had ever been married, we do not know. Yet from that confinement he wrote the most profound vision of marriage in the Bible. Why? Because this passage is really about something bigger than marriage. It is about Christ's love for His people. Whether you are married, widowed, single, or separated — this love story includes you. You are part of the Bride.

---

## 1. The Portrait (vv.22-24)

In the oldest manuscripts, verse 22 contains no verb. The "submit" idea is carried over from v.21 — "submitting to one another out of reverence for Christ." The wife's posture is rooted in the mutual submission of all believers.

The Greek *hypotassō* means voluntary self-ordering — not compliance forced from outside, but trust offered from inside. And "as to the Lord" sets the standard: the husband's will ought to align with God's.

Notice how Paul defines headship: "he is himself its Savior" (v.23). Whatever "head" means here, it is defined by rescue, not rule.

### Questions

1. Paul calls Christ "the Savior of the body" (v.23).  How has Christ been your Savior — not just at the cross, but in the daily realities of your life?

2. What is the difference between compliance (doing what you're told) and submission (making someone else's will your own)?  Can you think of a time you experienced that difference?

---

## 2. The Sacrifice (vv.25-27)

Paul commands husbands to love with *agapaō* — self-giving love, not feeling but action. The present tense means: *keep on loving*. The standard is Christ, who:

1. **Gave himself up** for her (v.25) — the atonement
2. **Sanctified her**, cleansing her by the washing of water with the word (v.26) — sanctification. The word *loutron* means "bath" — a full immersion, like the bridal *mikveh*.
3. **Will present her in splendor**, without spot or wrinkle (v.27) — glorification. The word for "wrinkle" — *rhytida* — appears only here in the entire New Testament. It means the lines that come with age and wear. Christ promises to remove every mark of sin *and* every mark of decay.

Christ did not wait for us to become beautiful. He loved us *into* beauty. He loved us *into* splendor. If you feel worn and wrinkled today, hear this: the One who loves you is not finished. Every ache, every limitation, every scar — He is working all of it toward glory.

### Questions

1. Christ promises to present His church "without spot or wrinkle."  What does that promise mean to you at this stage of life?

2. The word for "washing" means a full bath — not a quick rinse. What areas of your heart do you wish Christ would wash clean?

---

## 3. The Mystery (vv.28-32)

Paul uses two tender words for how Christ treats His church: *ektrephō* — "to nourish to maturity" (the same word for raising children) — and *thalpō* — "to warm, to brood over" (used of a mother warming a child against her body). These are not words of authority. They are words of tenderness.

"Hold fast" (*proskollaō*) literally means "to be glued to" — the same word used of Ruth clinging to Naomi and of Israel clinging to God. An unbreakable bond.

Then Paul steps back and marvels: "This mystery is profound — it refers to Christ and the church" (v.32). A *mystērion* is a hidden truth now revealed. Marriage was never just about two people. From Genesis 2, it was always a portrait of Christ's love for His church. We can finally see what was always there.

Christ left His Father's house — that is what the incarnation was. He held fast to His Bride — that is what the cross was. He will never let go — that is what eternity is. You are nourished. You are cherished. You are held fast — not by a human spouse who may be gone, but by Christ who said, "I will never leave you nor forsake you" (Hebrews 13:5).

### Questions

1. Paul says Christ "nourishes and cherishes" the church — using words for feeding and warming. In what ways has Christ nourished or warmed you recently?

2. "This mystery is profound." What is one thing about God's love that still amazes you, even after all these years?

---

## 4. The Ending That Is a Beginning (v.33)

Paul lands with a practical sentence: husband, love your wife. Wife, respect your husband. Grand theology, daily practice. But this passage is one chapter in the Bible's biggest love story — from Genesis 2 (the first marriage) through Hosea, Isaiah, Ezekiel, and the Song of Solomon, all the way to Revelation 19: "The marriage of the Lamb has come, and his Bride has made herself ready... Blessed are those who are invited to the marriage supper of the Lamb." You are invited. The invitation does not expire with age. It does not depend on your health. It requires only that you say yes to the Bridegroom. The Bible does not end with a funeral. It ends with a wedding feast. The best is not behind you. The best is ahead.

### Questions

1. The Bible begins with a marriage and ends with a marriage. What does it mean to you that God chose a wedding as the final image of eternity?
