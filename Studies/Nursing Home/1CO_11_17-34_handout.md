## Overview

Paul writes to a socially mixed church in status-obsessed Corinth (c. AD 53-55) — this is the earliest written record of the Lord's Supper. The church met in homes, where the Supper was joined to a full communal meal. Wealthy members arrived early and ate their fill in the good seats; poorer laborers and slaves came late and went hungry. They had imported Corinth's banqueting hierarchy into the one meal designed to dissolve rank — so it was no longer the *Lord's* supper but "his own" (v.21). Paul corrects them not with opinion but by returning to Jesus' own words at the institution (vv.23-26), then drawing the consequence: examine yourself, discern the body, and "wait for one another." The whole passage is framed by the repeated phrase "when you come together" — the gathering itself is the problem and the cure.

**Big idea:** The Lord's Supper joins us to Christ *and* to one another; to despise fellow believers while eating is to profane the meal and eat judgment on oneself.

## Info

### vv.17-22 — The Rebuke
- "Come together" recurs throughout — the assembly that should unite is dividing.
- "Divisions" (v.18, *schisma*) = a *tear*; the wealthy eat first and best while "those who have nothing" are humiliated.
- The sin isn't eating together — it's treating the church like a status banquet ("despise the church of God," v.22).

### vv.23-26 — The Tradition
- "I received… I also delivered" — Paul hands on Jesus' words, the source of authority.
- Bread = "my body… for you"; cup = "the new covenant in my blood" (echoes Jeremiah 31:31).
- "In remembrance" = active covenant renewal, not mere nostalgia.
- v.26 (the center): the meal *proclaims the Lord's death until he comes* — a sermon looking back to the cross and forward to his return.

### vv.27-32 — The Warning
- "Unworthy manner" (v.27) is an **adverb** — it condemns the *way* they ate (divisively), not unworthy *people*. No one is worthy in himself.
- "Discern the body" (v.29) — both the bread *and* the church; failing the poor is failing to discern the body.
- God's judgment (v.30) is real but fatherly: discipline that spares believers from condemnation (v.32).

### vv.33-34 — The Remedy
- "Wait for one another" — love as forbearance; serve the others first.
- Satisfy private hunger at home; let the gathering be governed by the body's communion, not individual consumption.

## Discussion

1. Paul repeats "when you come together" five times. What is he highlighting about the church's gathering?
2. What were the Corinthians actually doing wrong at the meal (vv.20-22), and why did it make Paul so indignant?
3. When Paul corrects them, where does he go (vv.23-26)? What does that teach us about how to fix church problems?
4. According to v.26, what does the Lord's Supper *do*? How should that shape the way we take communion?
5. To eat "in an unworthy manner" (v.27) — does that mean unworthy people should stay away? What is Paul really warning against?
6. Where do we subtly sort people by status, wealth, or usefulness in our own church life?
7. What would "wait for one another" (v.33) look like, concretely, among us this week — and is there someone you need to be reconciled with before the table?
