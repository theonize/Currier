## Overview

**Setting**
- Letter from Paul, written from Ephesus, c. AD 53-55; earliest written account of the Lord's Supper, predating the Gospels.
- Corinth: Roman boomtown, honor-shame culture, status competition. Church socially mixed — mostly poor ("not many… powerful," 1:26) plus a few wealthy patrons (Erastus, Gaius).
- Church met in homes. The Roman house — small formal *triclinium* (~9 reclining diners) vs. larger *atrium/*courtyard — physically split the rich (good food, good seats) from the poor (overflow, scraps).

**The problem**
- The Supper was joined to a full communal meal (the *agapē* love feast). Wealthy members arrived early, ate and drank their fill; laborers and slaves arrived late and went hungry (vv.21-22).
- They imported Corinth's banqueting hierarchy into the one meal designed to dissolve rank. Result: not the *Lord's* supper but "his own" (v.21).

**Author / structure**
- Paul as **tradent** — transmitter of received tradition (v.23, technical handing-on language). He corrects abuse by re-anchoring the meal in Jesus' institution.
- Three movements framed by an inclusio on **"come together"** (*synerchomai*, 5×):
  1. **Rebuke** (vv.17-22) — divisive eating; "I do not commend."
  2. **Tradition** (vv.23-26) — body, covenant, blood; the theological center.
  3. **Application** (vv.27-34) — examine yourself, discern the body, "wait for one another."
- Chiasm drives to **v.26**: the meal is *proclamation* — a visible sermon of Christ's death "until he comes."

**Big idea**
- The Lord's Supper unites the vertical (communion with Christ) and the horizontal (communion with his people). To despise fellow believers — especially the poor — while eating is to profane the meal and eat judgment on oneself.

**Supporting movements**
- The meal is covenantal: "new covenant in my blood" (v.25) invokes Jeremiah 31:31 + Sinai (Exodus 24:8).
- "The body" (v.29) is a double meaning: the bread *and* the church.
- Careless participation is serious — it invited real divine discipline (v.30), yet discipline aims at restoration, not condemnation (v.32).

## Info

### vv.17-19
- **synerchomai** (G4905, "come together") repeated 5× across the passage — the *assembly* itself is the locus of failure.
- "Not for the better but for the worse" — antithesis; the gathering meant to edify is doing harm.
- **schisma** (G4978, v.18, "tear/split") — same word as a torn garment; the body is being ripped.
- **hairesis** (G139, v.19, "faction") — root of "heresy"; self-chosen partisanship. The factions ironically expose who is "genuine."

### vv.20-22
- *kyriakon deipnon* ("the Lord's supper") vs. *idion deipnon* ("his own meal") — pointed antithesis (vv.20-21).
- Present-tense iteratives — "goes ahead," "goes hungry," "gets drunk" — durative; this is *habitual* abuse, not a one-off.
- "Despise the church of God and humiliate those who have nothing" (v.22) — the sin is treating Christ's body as a common civic banquet for status display.
- Four rapid rhetorical questions + emphatic refusal ("No, I will not") forming an inclusio with v.17.

### vv.23-25 (The Institution)
- "I **received**… I also **delivered**" — rabbinic transmission vocabulary; Paul hands on, he does not invent.
- **eucharisteō** (G2168, "gave thanks," v.24) — the verb that names the "Eucharist."
- "This **is** my body" — *estin* is the simple copula; centuries of debate hinge on whether it means identity, representation, or covenantal sign.
- **anamnēsis** (G364, "remembrance," vv.24-25) — active re-presentation / covenant renewal, not bare mental recall (cf. Passover *zikkaron*, Exodus 12:14).
- **diathēkē** (G1242, "covenant," v.25) — LXX rendering of *berith*; evokes Jeremiah 31:31's new covenant sealed in blood.
- *Textual note:* earliest mss read "my body which is **for you**"; "broken for you" is a later expansion (and the body was *not* broken, John 19:36).
- Present imperative *poieite* ("**keep** doing this") — ongoing observance, not one-time.

### v.26
- **Theological apex.** **katangellō** (G2605, "proclaim") — the Supper *preaches*; to eat is to announce Christ's death.
- "Until he comes" — sets the rite in the eschatological interval between the two advents; a meal "between the times."

### vv.27-28
- **anaxiōs** (G371, "in an unworthy manner," v.27) is an **adverb** — condemns the divisive *manner* of eating (vv.21-22), not unworthy *persons*. No one is "worthy" in himself. (Critical pastoral point — see Discussion.)
- **dokimazō** (G1381, "examine," v.28) — to assay metal for genuineness; honest self-testing before the table.
- "Guilty concerning the body and blood" — forensic, covenant-lawsuit idiom: liable, answerable.

### vv.29-30
- **diakrinō** (G1252, "discern," v.29) — to distinguish/evaluate rightly. "Without discerning **the body**" — famously ambiguous: the church (failing the poor of vv.21-22), the eucharistic body, or both (Pauline double entendre). Shorter reading "the body" is original.
- **krima** (G2917, vv.29,34) — "judgment/verdict," not damnation per se.
- "Weak… ill… died" (v.30) — ascending triad; real temporal discipline on a careless church.

### vv.31-32
- "If we judged ourselves… we would not be judged" — self-examination forestalls divine judgment (echoes wisdom: fear of the Lord as reverent self-judgment).
- v.32: discipline is **paternal mercy** — "so that we may not be condemned along with the world" (cf. Hebrews 12:5-11). Distinguishes the believer's discipline from the world's condemnation.

### vv.33-34
- **Remedy: "wait for one another."** Love as forbearance; mutual deference replaces self-service.
- "Let him eat at home" — satisfy private appetite privately; the assembly is governed by the body's communion, not the individual's consumption.
- "My brothers" (v.33) softens the rebuke into family appeal — closes the frame: not "for judgment."

## Discussion

### Observation
1. How many times does Paul say "when you come together"? What is he signaling by the repetition?
   - **Leader's note:** 5× (*synerchomai*). The *gathering* is the issue — the assembly is meant to unite, but theirs divides. Surfaces the inclusio structure (vv.17, 34).
2. What exactly were the Corinthians doing wrong at the meal (vv.20-22)?
   - **Leader's note:** Wealthy ate first/best while the poor went hungry — Roman banqueting hierarchy imported into the church. Use the *triclinium* vs. *atrium* picture from the Overview.
3. Where does Paul go to correct them (vv.23-26)? Whose words are these?
   - **Leader's note:** Not his own opinion but the *received* tradition of Jesus' institution. He fixes practice by returning to the source. Note "received… delivered."
4. What does Paul say the Supper actually *does* (v.26)?
   - **Leader's note:** It "proclaims the Lord's death until he comes." The meal is a sermon — backward to the cross, forward to the return.

### Interpretation
5. What does it mean to eat "in an unworthy manner" (v.27)? Does it mean unworthy *people* shouldn't come?
   - **Leader's note:** Key correction. *anaxiōs* is an **adverb** — the divisive, self-serving *manner*, not personal worth. The table is for the unworthy; the warning is against *how* they ate. Guard against driving timid believers from communion.
6. What is "the body" that must be discerned (v.29)?
   - **Leader's note:** Deliberately double — the bread *and* the gathered church. Failing to discern the body is precisely the sin of vv.21-22. Let the group sit with both senses.
7. How can God's judgment (v.30) and his discipline (v.32) both be loving?
   - **Leader's note:** v.32 frames it: discipline is fatherly correction that *spares* believers from being "condemned with the world." Restoration, not destruction.
8. Why does Paul ground a meal-etiquette problem in the deepest theology of the cross?
   - **Leader's note:** Because how we treat the weakest at the table is a true index of our communion with Christ. The horizontal and vertical are inseparable.

### Application
9. Where do *we* (subtly) sort people by status, wealth, usefulness, or politics in church life?
   - **Leader's note:** Pull from Application table — cliques, networking, favoritism, overlooked members. Aim for honest, specific self-examination.
10. What would "wait for one another" (v.33) look like concretely in our gatherings and meals?
    - **Leader's note:** Serve the late, the poor, the awkward *first*; refuse cliques. Apply to potlucks, small groups, hospitality, budgets.
11. How should this passage shape the way each of us approaches the communion table?
    - **Leader's note:** Self-examination + reconciliation before receiving (Matthew 5:23-24). Awareness of the body, not mechanical ritual.
12. Is there anyone in this body you've been content to overlook or look down on? What is one step toward reconciliation this week?
    - **Leader's note:** Move from diagnosis to action. Keep it personal and concrete; consider closing in prayer of self-examination.
