# The Family Around Him

## A Group Study on Mark 3:31-35

> And his mother and his brothers came, and standing outside they sent to him and called him.
> And a crowd was sitting around him, and they said to him, "Your mother and your brothers are outside, seeking you."
> And he answered them, "Who are my mother and my brothers?"
> And looking about at those who sat around him, he said, "Here are my mother and my brothers!
> For whoever does the will of God, he is my brother and sister and mother."

---

### Introduction

Mark wrote this Gospel for Christians in Rome who had been disowned by their own families for their faith. Many had been cut off — declared dead by their relatives — because they followed Jesus. Some had watched family members turn away during Nero's persecution, when Christians were arrested, tortured, and killed. These believers knew what it meant to lose a family.

So when Mark recorded this scene — Jesus' own mother and brothers standing outside, unable or unwilling to enter the house where he was teaching — his readers felt the sting of recognition. They had been on both sides of that door.

But Mark also recorded Jesus' answer. And that answer changed everything.

Jesus looked at the ordinary people sitting around him — fishermen, tax collectors, women of no social standing, the sick, the searching — and said: *"Here are my mother and my brothers."*

If his family could reject him and he could still have a family, then so could they. And so can you.

This passage answers one of the deepest questions a human being can ask: *If my family is far away — or gone — do I still have a family?*

Jesus says yes. And that family is sitting right here.

## Section 1: Outside and Inside

**Focus:** Mark 3:31-32 — *"And his mother and his brothers came, and standing outside they sent to him and called him."*

### Context / Setup
- In first-century Jewish culture, family = identity + economic safety net + social standing + religious community
- The fifth commandment ("Honor your father and mother") was treated as one of the weightiest commands in the Torah
- Joseph is absent from Jesus' public ministry — likely deceased.  Jesus, the firstborn son, is expected to lead the household
- Mark 3:21 is the backstory: his family came to "seize him" because they thought he had lost his mind — an intervention to restore family honor
- Capernaum houses excavated: rough basalt, ~15 ft square, opening onto courtyard.  Jesus is inside; family is 20 miles from Nazareth, standing outside

### Theological Anchor
- **Inside vs. outside** is a Markan theological marker (cf. Mark 4:11, "those outside")
- Physical geography tells the spiritual story: chosen presence matters more than bloodline
- Biological connection ≠ spiritual closeness
- The crowd begins as messengers; they will end as the message

### Language Notes
- **standing outside** (Greek: *exō stēkontes*) — Mark uses *exō* repeatedly as a theological, not just physical, marker
- **sitting around him** (Greek: *peri auton kathēmenous*) — the classic posture of discipleship; sitting at a teacher's feet (cf. Luke 10:39)

### Talking Points
- Family honor culture: mother's summons = non-negotiable.  The separation is stunning
- Mary and the brothers walked a full day — twenty miles of hills — and still can't get through
- The room is packed; the floor is full; there is literally no space for the family to enter
- The crowd is not passive scenery — they are the first ones to hear themselves redefined as family
- "Outside" is not just a location.  It is a spiritual condition

### For Us
- Many in this room know what it feels like when family is "outside" — far, busy, complicated, or gone
- The ache of family at a distance is real.  This passage does not minimize it
- But notice who is **inside** — the people who showed up, who are present, who share the room
- In Jesus' eyes, that matters more than a last name
- The brother or sister sitting next to you at meals may count for more than you have let yourself believe

### Teaching Tip
Do not rush past the ache of family being "outside." Many residents live with this daily.  Let the longing be named before the comfort is offered.

### Reflection Questions

1. Jesus' family traveled a full day to reach him, but they couldn't get through the crowd. Have you ever felt like family was close but somehow unreachable — or far away when you needed them most?

2. The crowd in this story started as messengers and ended up being called family. Who are the people in your life right now who "show up" — who are present with you day after day?

## Section 2: The Look

**Focus:** Mark 3:33-34 — *"And he answered them, 'Who are my mother and my brothers?' And looking about at those who sat around him, he said, 'Here are my mother and my brothers!'"*

### Context / Setup
- "Who are my mother and my brothers?" — in that culture, a borderline violation of the fifth commandment
- A theological earthquake, not a rhetorical flourish
- Mark alone preserves the detail: Jesus **looked** around the room before he spoke
- Then a command: "*Look!* Here are my mother and my brothers!"

### Theological Anchor
- Jesus' words do not merely **describe** his family — they **create** it
- The claim is performative: by speaking while looking, he *makes* these people family
- In the new kingdom, family is reconstituted around obedience, not biology
- The same Jesus who saw this room sees this room

### Language Notes
- **looking about** (Greek: *periblepsamenos*) — a slow, deliberate, sweeping gaze.  Uniquely Markan — 6 of 7 NT uses are in Mark (3:5 before healing; 5:32 seeking who touched him; 11:11 surveying the temple).  Every use marks a moment of intense, personal attention
- **Here are / Behold** (Greek: *ide*) — a command: "Look!" Jesus is pointing, not generalizing

### Talking Points
- The question would have shocked every listener: Jesus is not disowning his mother — he is redefining the category
- The slow look matters.  Not a glance — a gaze.  Every face was seen
- He could have said "those who do God's will are *like* my family." He didn't.  He said "Here **are** my mother and my brothers"
- Description vs. declaration: this is the moment he claims them, not the moment he compares them
- These were not impressive people.  They were not qualified.  They had not earned it.  They were just *there* — and he claimed them

### For Us
- The world looks past the elderly; you may feel invisible in a culture that worships speed, productivity, and youth
- Jesus did not do that.  He stopped.  He turned.  He looked — slowly, deliberately — at every face in that room
- The Jesus who looked at that crowd looks at you: not the version from thirty years ago, not the version your family remembers — **you**, right now, in this chair, with these limitations and this faith
- And he says the same thing: *Here is my family*

### Teaching Tip
Slow down on the word "looked." In a culture of hurried glances, residents often receive this detail as personal consolation.  The teacher's voice should match the pace of Jesus' gaze.

### Reflection Questions

1. Jesus looked at ordinary, unimpressive people and claimed them as his own. When in your life has someone truly *seen* you — not looked past you, but really seen you?

2. Jesus didn't say "those who do the will of God are *like* my family." He said "Here *are* my mother and my brothers." What's the difference?

## Section 3: Whoever

**Focus:** Mark 3:35 — *"For whoever does the will of God, he is my brother and sister and mother."*

### Context / Setup
- Three Greek details open the sentence up: "whoever," "does the will of God," and the conspicuous **omission of "father"**
- The door swings wide open and stays propped there
- "Sister" appears for the first time — the original scene didn't include Jesus' sisters.  He is expanding the category, not just describing it
- The final word is "mother" — the rhetorical climax, the highest honor in the list

### Theological Anchor
- **"Whoever"** — no age limit, no mobility requirement, no cognitive test, no pedigree check
- Doing God's will is the **evidence** of being in the family, not the **price** of admission
- God alone is Father.  No human substitute needed — or competing
- In Christ, you are not an orphan; you are adopted (Rom 8:15)

### Language Notes
- **whoever may do** (Greek: *hos an poiēsē*) — indefinite relative with subjunctive; deliberately open-ended
- **the will of God** (Greek: *to thelēma tou theou*) — not a static code; an active, living purpose
- **brother / sister / mother** — Jesus includes all three but *omits* "father" (cf. Mark 8:38; 11:25; 14:36 — Jesus reserves "Father" for God alone)
- **Abba** (Aramaic) — the intimate "Daddy" address.  Jesus' own word in Gethsemane (Mark 14:36); Paul says the Spirit puts that same word in your mouth (Rom 8:15)

### Talking Points
- Three Greek features: whoever, does God's will, no "father"
- "Whoever" has no fine print — this is the widest door in the Gospel
- "Doing God's will" in Mark = receiving the kingdom, following Jesus at cost, submitting to God's purposes even in pain
- From a care facility, doing God's will looks like: praying for a grandson, speaking kindly to a nurse, singing a hymn in the hallway, enduring pain without bitterness, forgiving the family member who doesn't visit enough
- Moses held up his hands while Israel fought (Exodus 17:11-12) — the intercessor in the wheelchair may be changing the course of the war
- No "father" on the list — because you already have one.  God alone

### For Us
- If you never knew your earthly father, or lost him long ago — you have a Father in heaven who chose you before the foundation of the world (Eph 1:4)
- If your mother is gone — God says: "Can a woman forget her nursing child? Even these may forget, yet I will not forget you" (Isa 49:15)
- If your siblings are distant — Jesus looks around this room and says: here are your brothers and sisters
- The same Aramaic word Jesus prayed in Gethsemane (*Abba*), the Spirit teaches you to pray
- **One more thing about Mary.** She stood outside in Mark 3.  She stood at the foot of the cross in John 19.  She prayed with the disciples in Acts 1.  Her journey from outside to inside happened over time — through suffering, through watching, through faith.  If someone you love is still outside, the door is never locked

### Teaching Tip
The Mary coda is often the section that most comforts residents.  Do not skip it.  Many carry years of prayer for a family member still "outside." The point is hope, not certainty, but hope is enough.

### Reflection Questions

1. "Whoever does the will of God" — what does doing God's will look like for you, right now, in your current situation? What is one way you are serving God today that you might not have recognized?

2. Jesus says "brother and sister and mother" but not "father" — because God alone is Father. What does it mean to you that God is your Father? How has that relationship sustained you?

3. Mary was "outside" in Mark 3 but "inside" by Acts 1. Is there someone in your life you're still hoping will come "inside"? What gives you patience to keep hoping?

### For Reflection

This week, try two things:

- **Look around your dining table, your hallway, your activity room, your Bible study group.** These are your brothers and sisters.  You did not choose each other — God put you together.  Treat the person next to you the way you would treat family.  Ask about their day.  Remember their name.  Pray for them before you fall asleep
- **If there is someone you love who feels far away** — a child who doesn't call, a friend who drifted, a family member who doesn't understand your faith — hold them before the Lord this week.  Mary stood outside in Mark 3, stood at the cross by Mark 15, and was praying in Acts 1.  Journeys take time.  Keep the door open

Jesus looked at a room full of imperfect people and claimed them as his own.  He did not wait for them to earn it.  He did not check their pedigree.  He looked at them — at their willingness to sit with him, to listen, to orient their lives around the will of God — and said, *"This is my family."*

That same invitation stands today.  Right here.  Right now.  For whoever.

### Key Terms at a Glance

| English (ESV) | Greek | Meaning |
|----------------|-------|---------|
| "Standing outside" | *exō stēkontes* | Physically and symbolically on the outside — Mark uses *exō* as a theological marker for those outside the circle of understanding (cf. 4:11) |
| "Sitting around him" | *peri auton... kathēmenous* | The posture of discipleship — sitting at a teacher's feet (cf. Luke 10:39; Acts 22:3) |
| "Looking about" | *periblepsamenos* | A slow, deliberate, sweeping gaze — uniquely Markan (6 of 7 NT uses are in Mark). Marks moments of solemn pronouncement. |
| "Here are" | *ide* | "Look! Behold!" — a command to pay attention. Jesus points and declares. |
| "Whoever does" | *hos an poiēsē* | Indefinite relative clause with subjunctive — deliberately open: "whoever may do." No restrictions. |
| "The will of God" | *to thelēma tou theou* | God's active purpose and moral direction — not a static code but a living relationship of obedience |
| "Brother" | *adelphos* | Literally "from the same womb" — Jesus redefines the womb as obedience, not biology |
| "Sister" | *adelphē* | First mention in the passage — not in the original scene. Jesus expands the categories beyond the situation. |
| "Mother" | *mētēr* | The rhetorical climax: Jesus began by seemingly questioning his own mother, now includes "mother" as the highest honor in the new family |

### Passage Structure at a Glance

```
THE SCENE (vv.31-32)
  → Family arrives, stands OUTSIDE
  → Crowd sits AROUND Jesus, inside
  → Crowd relays the message: "Your mother and brothers are seeking you"

THE QUESTION (v.33)
  → "Who are my mother and my brothers?"
  → Not a request for information — a redefinition

THE LOOK (v.34)
  → Jesus looks slowly around the room
  → Points to those sitting around him
  → "Here are my mother and my brothers!"

THE INVITATION (v.35)
  → "Whoever does the will of God"
  → "He is my brother and sister and mother"
  → No age limit. No fine print. Whoever.
```
