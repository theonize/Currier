# The Family Around Him

## A Group Study on Mark 3:31-35

> And his mother and his brothers came, and standing outside they sent to him and called him.
> And a crowd was sitting around him, and they said to him, "Your mother and your brothers are outside, seeking you."
> And he answered them, "Who are my mother and my brothers?"
> And looking about at those who sat around him, he said, "Here are my mother and my brothers!
> For whoever does the will of God, he is my brother and sister and mother."

---

### Introduction

Mark wrote this Gospel for Christians in Rome who had been disowned by their own families for their faith. Many had been cut off — declared dead by their relatives — because they followed Jesus. Some had watched family members turn away during Nero's persecution, when Christians were arrested, tortured, and killed. These believers knew what it meant to lose a family.

So when Mark recorded this scene — Jesus' own mother and brothers standing outside, unable or unwilling to enter the house where he was teaching — his readers felt the sting of recognition. They had been on both sides of that door.

But Mark also recorded Jesus' answer. And that answer changed everything.

Jesus looked at the ordinary people sitting around him — fishermen, tax collectors, women of no social standing, the sick, the searching — and said: *"Here are my mother and my brothers."*

If his family could reject him and he could still have a family, then so could they. And so can you.

This passage answers one of the deepest questions a human being can ask: *If my family is far away — or gone — do I still have a family?*

Jesus says yes. And that family is sitting right here.

---

## Section 1: Outside and Inside

**Focus:** Mark 3:31-32 — *"And his mother and his brothers came, and standing outside they sent to him and called him."*

### Notes

To understand how shocking this scene was, you need to know what family meant in Jesus' world.

In first-century Jewish culture, the family was everything. It was your identity, your economic safety net, your social standing, and your religious community — all rolled into one. The fifth commandment — "Honor your father and mother" — was not a suggestion. It was an absolute obligation. The rabbis taught that honoring parents was one of the weightiest commands in the entire Torah.

As the firstborn son, Jesus bore particular responsibilities. Joseph appears to have died by this point — he is absent from every Gospel account of Jesus' public ministry. That meant Jesus was expected to lead the household, provide for his widowed mother, carry on the family trade, and represent the family in public. His departure to become an itinerant preacher would have been seen as an abdication of duty. His family was not just missing him — they were humiliated by him.

Mark 3:21 tells us the backstory: Jesus' family had gone out to "seize him" because they thought he had lost his mind. In an honor-shame culture, a family member behaving erratically in public brought shame on the entire household. This was an intervention — an attempt to remove Jesus from public view and restore the family's reputation.

Now picture the scene. Archaeologists have excavated first-century houses in Capernaum — small rooms built of rough basalt stone, maybe fifteen feet square, opening onto a shared courtyard. Jesus is inside one of these rooms, packed with people sitting on the floor. His mother and brothers have walked twenty miles from Nazareth — a full day's journey through hilly terrain — and they cannot even get through the door. They stand outside and send word in through the crowd.

The physical layout tells the theological story. Jesus is *inside*, surrounded by people who have chosen to sit with him. His biological family is *outside*, unable to reach him. In a culture where family had automatic right of access — where a mother's summons would be obeyed without question — the separation is stunning.

And the crowd plays an unusual role. They are not just bystanders. They relay the message: "Your mother and your brothers are outside, seeking you." They begin as messengers. By the end of the passage, they will become the message.

**Why this matters for us:** Many of you know what it feels like when family is "outside." Maybe they live far away. Maybe they're busy. Maybe they don't visit as often as you wish. Maybe the relationship is complicated. The ache of family at a distance is real, and this passage does not minimize it.

But notice who is *inside* — the people who showed up. The people who are present. The people sitting together, listening, sharing a room. In Jesus' eyes, that mattered more than a last name.

### Reflection Questions

1. Jesus' family traveled a full day to reach him, but they couldn't get through the crowd. Have you ever felt like family was close but somehow unreachable — or far away when you needed them most?

2. The crowd in this story started as messengers and ended up being called family. Who are the people in your life right now who "show up" — who are present with you day after day?

## Section 2: The Look

**Focus:** Mark 3:33-34 — *"And he answered them, 'Who are my mother and my brothers?' And looking about at those who sat around him, he said, 'Here are my mother and my brothers!'"*

### Notes

Jesus' response would have been electrifying — and offensive.

"Who are my mother and my brothers?" In a culture where questioning your own mother's identity bordered on violating the fifth commandment, this was not a polite rhetorical device. It was a theological earthquake. Jesus was not asking because he didn't know who his mother was. He was dismantling the assumption that biological connection automatically equals spiritual closeness.

Then Mark records a detail that only he preserves. The Greek word is *periblepsamenos* — "having looked around." It appears seven times in the New Testament, and six of those are in Mark alone. It describes a slow, deliberate, sweeping gaze. Not a glance. Not a scan. Jesus turned and looked — really looked — at every face in that room.

Mark uses this same word when Jesus looks around at the Pharisees in anger before healing the withered hand (3:5). He uses it when Jesus looks around to see who touched his garment (5:32). He uses it when Jesus looks around the temple before departing (11:11). Every time, it marks a moment of intense, personal attention.

And then Jesus speaks. Not a theological lecture. A declaration: "Here are my mother and my brothers!"

The word *here* — in Greek, *ide*, "Look!" — is a command. Jesus is pointing. He is not making an abstract statement about community. He is looking at specific human beings and claiming them as family. His words don't just describe reality — they create it. By saying "Here is my family" while looking at them, he *makes* them family in the very act of speaking.

Think about what these people looked like. They were not impressive. They were not qualified. They had not earned this. They were just... there. Sitting around Jesus. Listening. Present.

And he claimed them.

**Why this matters for us:** The world has a way of looking past the elderly. You may feel invisible sometimes — overlooked in a culture that values speed, productivity, and youth. People walk past without really seeing.

Jesus didn't do that. He stopped. He turned. He looked — slowly, deliberately — at every person in that room. And he said, "You. You are mine."

That same Jesus sees you. Not the version of you from thirty years ago. Not the version your family remembers. You — right now, in this chair, in this room, with these limitations and this faith. He looks at you the way he looked at that crowd, and he says the same thing: *Here is my family.*

### Reflection Questions

1. Jesus looked at ordinary, unimpressive people and claimed them as his own. When in your life has someone truly *seen* you — not looked past you, but really seen you?

2. Jesus didn't say "those who do the will of God are *like* my family." He said "Here *are* my mother and my brothers." What's the difference?

## Section 3: Whoever

**Focus:** Mark 3:35 — *"For whoever does the will of God, he is my brother and sister and mother."*

### Notes

Three features of this verse deserve careful attention.

**First: "Whoever."** The Greek construction uses an indefinite pronoun with a subjunctive verb — *hos an poiēsē* — meaning "whoever may do." It is deliberately open-ended. There is no fine print. No age limit. No mobility requirement. No cognitive test. No social standing check. *Whoever.* That word swings the door of God's family wide open and props it there.

**Second: "Does the will of God."** This is the entry criterion — not bloodline, not church membership, not theological expertise. "Doing the will of God" in Mark's Gospel means receiving the kingdom as Jesus proclaims it, following him at personal cost, and submitting to God's purposes even when they are painful.

But notice: Jesus is not teaching salvation by works. He is describing what genuine faith looks like from the outside. Doing God's will is not the *price* of admission to his family — it is the *evidence* of having been admitted. As James would later write: "Faith apart from works is dead" (James 2:26).

And what does "doing God's will" look like from a care facility? It looks like praying from a wheelchair for a grandson who has wandered from the faith. It looks like speaking a kind word to a nurse who is having a hard day. It looks like singing a hymn in the hallway — even if your voice cracks. It looks like enduring pain without bitterness. It looks like forgiving a family member who doesn't visit enough. It looks like sitting in this room, right now, opening a Bible, and saying, "Lord, I'm still here. Use me."

Moses held up his hands while Israel fought the battle (Exodus 17:11-12). The intercessor in the wheelchair may be holding up hands that change the course of the war.

**Third: "Brother and sister and mother" — but not "father."** Jesus conspicuously omits "father" from his list. This is not an accident. Throughout Mark's Gospel, Jesus reserves the title "Father" for God alone (Mark 8:38; 11:25; 14:36). In the new family, God is the Father. No human substitute needed.

If you never knew your earthly father, or lost him long ago — Jesus says you have a Father in heaven who chose you before the foundation of the world (Ephesians 1:4). If your mother is gone, God says: "Can a woman forget her nursing child? Even these may forget, yet I will not forget you" (Isaiah 49:15). If your siblings are distant, Jesus looks around this room and says: here are your brothers and sisters.

Paul writes: "You have received the Spirit of adoption as sons, by whom we cry, 'Abba! Father!'" (Romans 8:15). *Abba* — the Aramaic word for "Daddy" — is the very word Jesus used in Gethsemane (Mark 14:36). The same intimate address the Son uses with the Father, the Spirit enables you to use. You are not an orphan. You are a child with a name, a place, a family, and a future.

**One more thing — about Mary.** Jesus' mother stood outside in Mark 3. But the story doesn't end there. She stood at the foot of the cross in John 19. She prayed with the disciples in the upper room in Acts 1. Mary's journey from "outside" to "inside" happened over time — through suffering, through watching, through faith. If someone you love is still "outside," there is hope. Mary's story says: the door is never locked.

### Reflection Questions

1. "Whoever does the will of God" — what does doing God's will look like for you, right now, in your current situation? What is one way you are serving God today that you might not have recognized?

2. Jesus says "brother and sister and mother" but not "father" — because God alone is Father. What does it mean to you that God is your Father? How has that relationship sustained you?

3. Mary was "outside" in Mark 3 but "inside" by Acts 1. Is there someone in your life you're still hoping will come "inside"? What gives you patience to keep hoping?

### For Reflection

This week, try two things:

**First:** Look around your dining table, your hallway, your activity room, your Bible study group. These are your brothers and sisters. You did not choose each other — God put you together. Treat the person next to you the way you would treat family. Ask about their day. Remember their name. Pray for them before you fall asleep.

**Second:** If there is someone you love who feels far away — a child who doesn't call, a friend who drifted, a family member who doesn't understand your faith — hold them before the Lord this week. Mary stood outside in Mark 3. She stood at the cross by Mark 15. She was praying in the upper room in Acts 1. Journeys take time. Keep the door open.

Jesus looked at a room full of imperfect people and claimed them as his own. He did not wait for them to earn it. He did not check their pedigree. He looked at them — at their willingness to sit with him, to listen, to orient their lives around the will of God — and said, *"This is my family."*

That same invitation stands today. Right here. Right now. For whoever.

### Key Terms at a Glance

| English (ESV) | Greek | Meaning |
|----------------|-------|---------|
| "Standing outside" | *exō stēkontes* | Physically and symbolically on the outside — Mark uses *exō* as a theological marker for those outside the circle of understanding (cf. 4:11) |
| "Sitting around him" | *peri auton... kathēmenous* | The posture of discipleship — sitting at a teacher's feet (cf. Luke 10:39; Acts 22:3) |
| "Looking about" | *periblepsamenos* | A slow, deliberate, sweeping gaze — uniquely Markan (6 of 7 NT uses are in Mark). Marks moments of solemn pronouncement. |
| "Here are" | *ide* | "Look! Behold!" — a command to pay attention. Jesus points and declares. |
| "Whoever does" | *hos an poiēsē* | Indefinite relative clause with subjunctive — deliberately open: "whoever may do." No restrictions. |
| "The will of God" | *to thelēma tou theou* | God's active purpose and moral direction — not a static code but a living relationship of obedience |
| "Brother" | *adelphos* | Literally "from the same womb" — Jesus redefines the womb as obedience, not biology |
| "Sister" | *adelphē* | First mention in the passage — not in the original scene. Jesus expands the categories beyond the situation. |
| "Mother" | *mētēr* | The rhetorical climax: Jesus began by seemingly questioning his own mother, now includes "mother" as the highest honor in the new family |

### Passage Structure at a Glance

```
THE SCENE (vv.31-32)
  → Family arrives, stands OUTSIDE
  → Crowd sits AROUND Jesus, inside
  → Crowd relays the message: "Your mother and brothers are seeking you"

THE QUESTION (v.33)
  → "Who are my mother and my brothers?"
  → Not a request for information — a redefinition

THE LOOK (v.34)
  → Jesus looks slowly around the room
  → Points to those sitting around him
  → "Here are my mother and my brothers!"

THE INVITATION (v.35)
  → "Whoever does the will of God"
  → "He is my brother and sister and mother"
  → No age limit. No fine print. Whoever.
```
