### Introduction

Moses is 120 years old.  He has led this people for forty years through a wilderness, and he knows that he himself will not cross the Jordan with them.  He is going to die on a mountain looking into a country he will never enter.  And before he dies, he gathers the entire nation — almost all of them young, most of them with no personal memory of Egypt — and he preaches the longest sermon in the Bible.  We call it Deuteronomy.

In the heart of that sermon, in the sixth chapter, comes a passage so important that observant Jews to this day recite it every morning and every evening — and pray that its words will be the last words on their lips when they die.  Jewish tradition calls these verses *the Shema*, after the first Hebrew word: *Sh'ma* — "Hear."

When a teacher of the law later asked Jesus, "Which is the greatest commandment?" — Jesus quoted these verses (Mark 12:29-30).  He called them the first and greatest.  Not one of the greatest.  *The* greatest.

These nine verses give us:

- **A confession** about who God is (v.4).
- **A command** about how to love Him (v.5).
- **A method** for keeping that love alive across the years and across the generations (vv.6-9).

For all of us — and especially for those of us who have walked with the Lord for many decades — this passage gathers up the whole life of faith into one breath.  It says: *Hear.  There is one Lord.  Love Him with everything.  Keep Him on your heart.  Pass Him on to your children.*  Nothing about the Christian life is more central than this.  Nothing about it is more simple.  And nothing about it is more demanding.

## Section 1: Hear, O Israel — The One Lord

**Focus:** Deuteronomy 6:4 — *"Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God, the LORD is one."*

### Context / Setup
- Moses is speaking to a people about to enter a land filled with many gods — Baal, Asherah, Molech, dozens of others
- Their neighbors believed in a god of rain, a god of crops, a god of war, a god of childbirth — *parceling life out among many lords*
- Into that crowded sky, Moses says one word first — *Hear* — and then one sentence: *the LORD is one*
- The Hebrew word for "hear" (*shema*) does not just mean "listen with the ears."  It means *listen so as to obey*.  In Hebrew there is no separate word for "obey" — *to truly hear* is *to do what was said*

### Theological Anchor
- Before God commands anything, He tells us *who He is*
- Right belief about God is the foundation of right love for God
- "One" means He has no rival, no competitor, no equal — and no part of His character contradicts another part
- The God who delivered them from Egypt is the same God who feeds them in the wilderness, the same God who will lead them in the land, the same God who will love them in old age

### Language Notes
- **Hear** (Hebrew: *shema* — שְׁמַע) — listen, attend, obey; the most important word in the Old Testament for the faithful response to God
- **LORD** (Hebrew: *YHWH* — יהוה) — God's covenant name, "the One who is," revealed to Moses at the burning bush
- **our God** (Hebrew: *Elohenu*) — *our* God; not a god you choose like a coat, but a God who has bound Himself to *us*
- **one** (Hebrew: *echad* — אֶחָד) — one, single, unique, undivided

### Talking Points
- The first word is not *do* — it is *hear*.  Religion does not begin with our action; it begins with God speaking and us listening
- Moses calls Israel by name — "*O Israel*" — the way a father calls a son to attention
- "The LORD is one" can be heard two ways, both true: *He is the only God*, and *He is undivided in Himself* — He does not contradict Himself, change His mind, or play favorites
- Pagan neighbors had to keep many gods happy; Israel had to please only One — and that One had already chosen to love them first
- For people who have lived through wars, depressions, deaths, and decades of change — the steadiness of the *one* unchanging Lord is the deepest comfort there is

### For Us
- Many of you have spent your whole life learning to *hear*.  The world is loud — the radio, the TV, the worries, the news of children and grandchildren.  Underneath all of it, one Voice has been speaking: *the LORD your God, the LORD is one*
- In old age, much gets stripped away.  Hearing fails.  Mobility fails.  Friends die.  Memories blur.  But *one* thing does not change: *the LORD is one*
- You may have served Him faithfully for sixty, seventy, eighty years.  You have known Him in joy and you have known Him in grief.  All of those seasons were the same Lord.  *The LORD is one*
- And when the day comes that you cannot remember much else — when the names of children and the days of the week begin to slip — these few words will still be true: *Hear.  The LORD our God, the LORD is one*
- Many saints across the centuries have died with the Shema on their lips.  It is a worthy first prayer in the morning and a worthy last prayer at night

### Reflection Questions

1. Across the long years of your life, where have you most clearly seen that the LORD is *one* — that He has been the same God in every season?
2. What does it mean for you, *today*, to *hear* before you *do*?  What is the Lord asking you to listen to right now?

## Section 2: Love the LORD with All

**Focus:** Deuteronomy 6:5-6 — *"You shall love the LORD your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your might.  And these words that I command you today shall be on your heart."*

### Context / Setup
- After confession (*who God is*) comes command (*what we owe Him*) — and the command is *love*
- This is not a command to *feel* a certain way; it is a command to *give* the whole self to God
- Moses names three spheres — heart, soul, might — and puts the word "all" in front of each one
- When Jesus later quoted this verse, He added a fourth — *mind* (Mark 12:30) — because the Hebrew "heart" already included thinking; He was unfolding what was always there

### Theological Anchor
- Love for God is not a feeling we manufacture; it is a loyalty we offer in response to His love for us
- No part of the self is exempt — God claims our affections, our wills, our memories, our resources, our weaknesses, and our strengths
- The "all" is not a measurement we have to achieve perfectly — it is a *direction* every faculty turns toward Him
- God's words are kept *on the heart*, not just *in the head*; truth that is only known but not loved has not yet done its work

### Language Notes
- **love** (Hebrew: *ahav* — אָהַב) — loyal, committed love; in the ancient world this word was used in covenant treaties for *unbreakable allegiance*, but it never stopped meaning real affection too
- **heart** (Hebrew: *levav* — לֵבָב) — the inner person; will, mind, intention, emotion all together
- **soul** (Hebrew: *nephesh* — נֶפֶשׁ) — the whole living self, including the body, the breath, the appetites, the life
- **might** (Hebrew: *me'od* — מְאֹד) — literally "very-much-ness"; everything you've got, including resources and strength
- **on your heart** (v.6) — not "in" the heart but *on* it, like words written on a tablet that lies open before you

### Talking Points
- Three Hebrew words, all preceded by *bekhol* — "with all" — pile up to mean: *every faculty, every capacity, nothing held back*
- Heart, soul, might is not a checklist of three boxes to tick — it is the picture of a whole self surrendered
- Notice the order in v.6 — "these words… shall be **on your heart**" comes *before* "you shall teach them to your children" (v.7).  *The Word must be on us before it can be passed through us*
- The ancient rabbis said that *me'od* meant "with all your money" — because possessions reveal whose we are
- Jesus did not invent the love command; He revealed its full measure.  And then He fulfilled it — perfectly loving the Father with His whole heart, soul, mind, and strength on our behalf

### For Us
- Many of you spent decades doing — working, parenting, building, serving.  Love showed up in calluses and casseroles, in tuition checks and prayer lists, in long marriages and longer faithfulness
- Now the season has changed.  The hands shake.  The list is shorter.  But love does not retire
- *Love with all your heart* — when the body cannot do much, the heart can still adore.  Adoration may be the most honest work of late life
- *Love with all your soul* — every breath that remains is a breath given back to the One who first breathed it into you
- *Love with all your might* — your *might* today may look very small.  A whispered prayer.  A held hand.  A faithful Bible kept open on a side table.  In God's economy these are the *all* of a faithful life
- "On your heart" — there are saints who can no longer find their way to a familiar room but who can still pray the Lord's Prayer.  The words *on the heart* outlast the words in the head.  This is why we must keep storing them up while we can

### Reflection Questions

1. Where in your life right now is it easiest to love God?  Where is it hardest?  What do you think He is teaching you in the harder place?
2. What words of Scripture, hymns, or prayers are *on your heart* — words you could still say if everything else were taken away?  What does it tell you that those particular words have stayed?

## Section 3: Pass It On

**Focus:** Deuteronomy 6:7-9 — *"You shall teach them diligently to your children, and shall talk of them when you sit in your house, and when you walk by the way, and when you lie down, and when you rise.  You shall bind them as a sign on your hand, and they shall be as frontlets between your eyes.  You shall write them on the doorposts of your house and on your gates."*

### Context / Setup
- After confession (v.4) and command (v.5) and internalization (v.6) comes *transmission* — what is on your heart must be passed to the next heart
- Moses names every place and every time of daily life — sitting, walking, lying down, rising; on hand, on forehead, on doorpost, on gate
- The point is not religious activity at scheduled times — it is *saturation*: the Word of God woven into every posture, every hour, every threshold of life
- Faithful Jews still wear small leather boxes (*tefillin*) on hand and forehead and post small scrolls (*mezuzot*) on their doorposts because of this passage

### Theological Anchor
- Faith is generational; the covenant is meant to flow from grandfathers to fathers to children to grandchildren
- The home — not the temple, not the school — is the primary classroom of faith
- Faith that is only kept private dies with one generation; faith that is *spoken*, *worn*, and *posted* lives on
- God's words are meant to govern the inside of us (heart), the actions of our bodies (hand), the focus of our attention (eyes), the life of our home (doorpost), and our public life (gates)

### Language Notes
- **teach diligently** (Hebrew: *shinnen* — שָׁנַן) — literally *sharpen, whet*; like sharpening a knife by repeated strokes.  Teach by *repeating until the words cut into the listener*
- **doorposts** (Hebrew: *mezuzot* — מְזוּזוֹת) — the side posts of a home's front door; the threshold of family life
- **gates** (Hebrew: *she'arim* — שְׁעָרִים) — the city gates, where business was done and judges sat; the public square
- **sign on your hand** — a marker on what you *do* — your work, your action
- **frontlets between your eyes** — a marker on what you *see and think* — your attention, your imagination

### Talking Points
- *Sit, walk, lie down, rise* covers every position of the day; the four together mean *all the time, in every posture*
- *Hand* and *eyes* together mean *all you do and all you see* — your work and your gaze
- *Doorposts* and *gates* together mean *home and city* — your private life and your public life
- The vision is total: there is no place where God's Word does not belong
- Notice who is responsible: *you* — the parent, the grandparent, the elder.  Faith was not outsourced to professionals.  It was the household's primary work
- Children learn faith less from formal instruction and more from *what they hear at the table, in the car, at bedtime, and at the door* — the small moments that fill up the day
- Many adult believers in America today became Christians because of a *grandmother* who prayed and spoke the name of Jesus in their hearing.  This is not sentiment — it is exactly what Deuteronomy 6 commanded

### For Us
- Many of you are in a season where you no longer raise children — but children and grandchildren still belong to your prayers and your words
- A grandmother who prays the Shema over a granddaughter she rarely sees is doing the very work commanded in Deuteronomy 6
- A grandfather who tells a grandson, *"Let me tell you what God has done for me,"* is teaching the way Moses commanded — *when you sit, when you walk*
- A short note, a phone call, a remembered birthday card with a verse on it — these are doorposts.  Small wood; large effect
- Some of you cannot reach grandchildren — by distance or by fracture.  Pray for them by name.  Speak God's words over them in your room.  The Lord who hears in heaven counts those words
- The legacy of a faithful elder is not money, not property, not even memory.  It is *the words on the heart that get passed to the next heart*.  That is what outlasts a casket
- Jesus' first disciples were taught Deuteronomy 6 from infancy by parents who took these verses seriously.  Without that household catechism there would be no Christian church today

### Reflection Questions

1. Who taught *you* about the Lord in your earliest years?  What words or stories from that teaching are still alive in you today?
2. Who are the children, grandchildren, neighbors, or younger believers in *your* life right now?  In what small ways could you "talk of these things" with them in this season?

### For Reflection

Take three things home from Deuteronomy 6 this week:

**First — Hear before you do.**  Each morning, before the day's worries take over, take a quiet moment to *hear*.  Read a verse.  Pray the Shema: *"Hear, O Israel: the LORD our God, the LORD is one."*  Let the *one* unchanging God set the tone for the whole day.

**Second — Love with all of what you have today.**  Do not measure your love for God by what you used to do.  Measure it by what you give Him *now* — the small prayers, the patient suffering, the kind word, the open Bible.  In God's economy, the widow's two coins were *all* she had.  That was the *all* He praised.

**Third — Speak the words.**  Find one person — a child, a grandchild, a friend, a nurse, the believer in the next chair — and say something true about God out loud this week.  *"The Lord has been faithful to me."*  *"God is good."*  *"He has never let me go."*  Doorposts do not need to be elaborate.  They just need to be *up*.

The God who spoke these words to Moses on the plains of Moab is the same God who speaks them to you today.  *Hear, O Israel: the LORD our God, the LORD is one.*

That is the first word of the morning.  That is the last word of the night.  That is the song of every faithful life — from cradle to grave, and into glory.
