# James 1:1-11 - Trials and Temptations — Study Handout

> **1** James, a servant of God and of the Lord Jesus Christ, To the twelve tribes in the Dispersion: Greetings.
> **2** Count it all joy, my brothers, when you meet trials of various kinds,
> **3** for you know that the testing of your faith produces steadfastness.
> **4** And let steadfastness have its full effect, that you may be perfect and complete, lacking in nothing.
> **5** If any of you lacks wisdom, let him ask God, who gives generously to all without reproach, and it will be given him.
> **6** But let him ask in faith, with no doubting, for the one who doubts is like a wave of the sea that is driven and tossed by the wind.
> **7** For that person must not suppose that he will receive anything from the Lord;
> **8** he is a double-minded man, unstable in all his ways.
> **9** Let the lowly brother boast in his exaltation,
> **10** and the rich in his humiliation, because like a flower of the grass he will pass away.
> **11** For the sun rises with its scorching heat and withers the grass; its flower falls, and its beauty perishes.  So also will the rich man fade away in the midst of his pursuits.
>
> — *English Standard Version (ESV)*

---

## Overview

James writes as "a servant of God and of the Lord Jesus Christ" (v.1) — traditionally James, the Lord's brother and leader of the Jerusalem church — to Jewish-Christian believers scattered outside Palestine, a poor and pressured community.  He opens not with comfort but with a command: "count it all joy" when trials come (v.2).  This is a reasoned reckoning of what trials produce, not a demand for happy feelings.  From there the passage moves in three linked steps: trials mature faith into completeness (vv.2-4); the wisdom needed to navigate them must be asked of God in undivided faith (vv.5-8); and the world's verdict on rich and poor gets overturned, since wealth fades like grass in the desert wind (vv.9-11).

**Big idea:** God matures faith through trials, supplies wisdom to those who ask him wholeheartedly, and reverses the world's judgment of status and wealth.

---

## Info

### v.1
- "Servant" (Greek *doulos*) — no title claimed, only total belonging to God and Christ; echoes the OT "servant of the LORD" (Moses, David, prophets).
- "Twelve tribes in the Dispersion" — the readers as eschatological, scattered Israel; likely written early (c. AD 45-49), possibly the oldest NT book.
- James does not appeal to being Jesus's brother — a notable humility.

### v.2
- "Count" (*hēgēsasthe*) is a deliberate evaluation, not an emotional performance.
- Sound-play: "greetings" (*chairein*, v.1) → "joy" (*charan*, v.2) — the letter's own opening becomes its theme.
- "Trials" (*peirasmos*) is the same word James later uses for inward temptation (vv.13-14).

### v.3
- "Testing" (*dokimion*) names the proving process — the image of assaying precious metal (cf. Malachi 3:3).
- "Steadfastness" (*hypomonē*) is active endurance, "remaining under" a load — not passive patience.

### v.4
- "Perfect" (*teleios*) means mature and whole, not sinless — the same word behind Matthew 5:48.
- The phrase "lacking nothing" hooks by wordplay into v.5's "lacks wisdom" — a deliberate seam.

### v.5
- Wisdom is to be *asked for*, recalling Solomon's request (1 Kings 3:9-12).
- God gives "generously" (*haplōs*, single-heartedly) and "without reproach" — unlike the grudging patrons of the ancient world.

### v.6-8
- "Doubting" (*diakrinomenos*) is inner division, not honest uncertainty — asking God while secretly trusting something else.
- "Double-minded" (*dipsychos*, "two-souled") may be James's own coinage; it reappears at 4:8 as the very condition the letter calls believers to repent of.
- The doubter is pictured as a wave "driven and tossed" by the wind — motion without direction.

### v.9-10
- "Lowly" (*tapeinos*) and "humiliation" (*tapeinōsis*) are the same root — the rich receive exactly the status the poor are lifted from.
- Echoes Hannah's Song (1 Sam 2:7-8) and Mary's Magnificat (Luke 1:52) — God's recurring reversal of worldly status.

### v.11
- "Scorching heat" (*kausōn*) likely names the sirocco, the desert's hot east wind, not just the sun.
- Near-verbatim echo of Isaiah 40:6-8 ("all flesh is grass... the flower fades") — the rich man's glory is as fleeting as a wildflower.

## Discussion

1. What word in v.1 does James use to describe himself, and what does it tell us about how he saw his own authority?
2. Why does James command believers to "count" trials as joy rather than simply expecting them to feel joyful?
3. What is the difference between the "doubting" James condemns (vv.6-8) and honest, weak faith?
4. Why does James pair the doubter's instability with the rich man's fading glory — what do a storm-tossed wave and a scorched flower have in common?
5. Where in your own life are you tempted to escape a trial rather than let it finish its work in you?
6. When you lack wisdom for a decision, is prayer your first move or your last resort — and why?
7. Does your sense of worth depend on anything that could be "scorched" overnight — income, title, reputation? What would be left if it were?
