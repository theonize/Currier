# Matthew 7:24-29 - Wise and Foolish Builders

## Verses
> **24** "Everyone then who hears these words of mine and does them will be like a wise man who built his house on the rock.
>
> **25** And the rain fell, and the floods came, and the winds blew and beat on that house, but it did not fall, because it had been founded on the rock.
>
> **26** And everyone who hears these words of mine and does not do them will be like a foolish man who built his house on the sand.
>
> **27** And the rain fell, and the floods came, and the winds blew and beat against that house, and it fell, and great was the fall of it."
>
> **28** And when Jesus finished these sayings, the crowds were astonished at his teaching,
>
> **29** for he was teaching them as one who had authority, and not as their scribes.
>
> — *English Standard Version (ESV)*

---

## Overview

This parable is the deliberate **conclusion of the Sermon on the Mount** (Matthew 5-7).  After a chain of either/or images — two gates, two trees, two confessions — Jesus ends with two builders.  Both hear the same teaching, both build a house, and both are struck by the identical storm of rain, floods, and wind.  Only two things differ: the **foundation** (rock or sand) and the **outcome** (the house stands or collapses).  The single decisive variable is whether the hearer *does* what Jesus says.  Set in agrarian Galilee, where flash floods routinely swept houses off the dry wadi floor, the picture was vivid and sobering — a collapsed house meant ruin, even death.  Behind it lies Israel's "two ways" tradition (Deuteronomy 30; Psalm 1; Proverbs 9), now re-centered on Jesus.  The narrator's closing note (vv.28-29) reveals the real scandal: Jesus taught with inherent **authority**, "not as their scribes," making obedience to *his own words* the line between survival and ruin in the final storm.

**Big idea:** A life endures the storm of testing and judgment only if built on the rock of hearing *and doing* Jesus' words; hearing without doing is fatal self-deception.

---

## Info

### v.24
- **"these words of mine"** = the whole Sermon (chs. 5-7).  Jesus places his own words where Israel's wisdom put Torah — an implicit claim to divine authority.
- **"hears... and does"** — **ἀκούει** + **ποιεῖ**.  To truly "hear" God is to obey (the Shema, Deuteronomy 6:4). Doing is the hinge.
- **"wise"** — **φρόνιμος**: practical good sense, skill in living (cf. the wise virgins, Matthew 25:2).
- **"Everyone then"** ties back to 7:21-23 ("Lord, Lord" / "I never knew you"); there is no neutral third option.
- **Rock** — anticipates Jesus' promise to build his church "on this rock" (Matthew 16:18).

### v.25
- Three forces — rain, floods, winds — a complete, no-mercy test that comes to *both* houses.
- **"had been founded"** is a pluperfect: the foundation was laid *before* the storm. The storm reveals what earlier obedience established; it does not create it.
- The wise house is delivered *through* the storm, not *from* it.

### v.26
- An exact mirror of v.24, changing only "does **not** do" and "**foolish**" (**μωρός** — dull, senseless).
- Both builders hear the *same* words; only the doing differs.
- **Sand** — the loose, level wadi floor: easier to build on, but shifting.

### v.27
- The same storm, told in near-identical words — isolating the one variable that matters.
- **"great was the fall of it"** — the heaviest word lands last; the sentence collapses as the house does.
- The foolish builder was equally diligent; his house looked just as finished.  Effort without obedience is wasted.

### vv.28-29
- "When Jesus finished these sayings" is the **first of five** such formulas that structure Matthew's Gospel (7:28; 11:1; 13:53; 19:1; 26:1) — Jesus as the new Moses.
- The crowds were **astonished** (ἐξεπλήσσοντο) because he taught with **authority** (ἐξουσία) — "I say to you" — unlike the scribes, who only cited tradition.
- "*Their* scribes" quietly distances Jesus and the community from the religious establishment.

## Discussion

1. What do the two builders share, and what is the one thing that finally separates them (vv.24-27)?
2. Both houses face the very same storm.  What does that tell us about trials and judgment in the Christian life?
3. What are "these words of mine" (vv.24, 26), and what is Jesus claiming by making *his* words the foundation?
4. Why does Jesus contrast "wise" and "foolish" rather than, say, "strong" and "weak" builders? What kind of wisdom is in view?
5. The foolish builder's house also looked finished.  Where might we be tempted to mistake a respectable surface for a solid foundation?
6. Where do you currently *know* something Jesus commands but are not yet *doing* it — and what would one concrete step of obedience look like this week?
7. How do the crowd's astonishment and the contrast with "their scribes" (vv.28-29) shape the way we should receive Jesus' words today?
