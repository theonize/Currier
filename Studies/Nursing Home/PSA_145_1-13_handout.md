# Exegetical Analysis of Psalms 145:1-13 - I will extol you, my King — Study Handout

## Verses

> **1** I will extol you, my God and King, and bless your name forever and ever.
>
> **2** Every day I will bless you and praise your name forever and ever.
>
> **3** Great is the LORD, and greatly to be praised, and his greatness is unsearchable.
>
> **4** One generation shall commend your works to another, and shall declare your mighty acts.
>
> **5** On the glorious splendor of your majesty, and on your wondrous works, I will meditate.
>
> **6** They shall speak of the might of your awesome deeds, and I will declare your greatness.
>
> **7** They shall pour forth the fame of your abundant goodness and shall sing aloud of your righteousness.
>
> **8** The LORD is gracious and merciful, slow to anger and abounding in steadfast love.
>
> **9** The LORD is good to all, and his mercy is over all that he has made.
>
> **10** All your works shall give thanks to you, O LORD, and all your saints shall bless you!
>
> **11** They shall speak of the glory of your kingdom and tell of your power,
>
> **12** to make known to the children of man your mighty deeds, and the glorious splendor of your kingdom.
>
> **13** Your kingdom is an everlasting kingdom, and your dominion endures throughout all generations. [The LORD is faithful in all his words and kind in all his works.]
>
> -- *English Standard Version (ESV)*

---

## Overview

Psalm 145 is the only psalm titled **תְּהִלָּה** (*tehillah*, "Praise") — the singular of the Hebrew name for the whole Psalter — and David's last superscribed psalm, the doorway into the closing Hallel (Pss 146-150). It is an alphabetic acrostic: each verse opens with the next Hebrew letter, signaling praise "from A to Z." Verses 1-13 move by widening circles — from one worshiper's lips (v.1), to one generation teaching the next (v.4), to the saints (v.10), to all creation (v.9), to eternity (v.13). At the structural center sits the Exodus 34:6 credo (vv.8-9): gracious, merciful, slow to anger, abounding in steadfast love. David, a king, opens by bowing to the true King — and ends declaring a kingdom that outlasts every throne.

**Big idea:** YHWH alone is the everlasting King, and because his greatness is grounded in covenant mercy, his praise must be comprehensive, daily, and passed from generation to generation.

## Info

### vv.1-2
- **אֲרוֹמִמְךָ** (*aromimkha*), "I will extol/lift high"; "my God and King" — royal-coronation language redirected to YHWH.
- "Every day... forever and ever" — daily *tamid* rhythm plus eternal horizon.

### v.3
- "Great... greatly... greatness" piles one root for emphasis.
- "Unsearchable" (*ein cheqer*) — God's greatness can never be exhausted (cf. Rom 11:33).

### v.4
- **דּוֹר לְדוֹר** (*dor ledor*), "generation to generation" — faith is handed off, not freshly invented each cycle (Deut 6:6-9).

### v.5
- Triple-stack "splendor of glory of majesty"; "I will meditate" (*siach*) — filled contemplation, a wisdom discipline.

### vv.6-7
- Praise overflows into testimony; **זֵכֶר** (*zeker*, "fame") travels by speech in an honor culture.

### vv.8-9
- **Chiastic center**, citing Exodus 34:6 — the OT's most-repeated credo of grace.
- "Slow to anger" = lit. "long of nostrils"; **רַחֲמִים** (*rachamim*) from "womb" — maternal compassion.
- "Good to all" — Israel's King is the universal Sovereign.

### vv.10-12
- Personified creation + the saints (*chasidim*) proclaim the kingdom to "the children of man" — universal audience.

### v.13
- "Everlasting kingdom" — political theology for a people without a throne; the bracketed **nun** line is restored from the Dead Sea Scrolls, LXX, and Peshitta.

## Discussion

1. Follow the "I will" and "they shall" verbs — how does praise move from one mouth (v.1) to all generations (v.13)?
2. List the words for God's greatness in vv.3-7. Why might the psalm pile them up, and why use an A-to-Z acrostic?
3. The literal center is vv.8-9. What does it say about God, and why place mercy at the center of a psalm about greatness?
4. Why does David, a king, call YHWH "my God and King"? What does that say about every throne we sit on?
5. What does "your kingdom is an everlasting kingdom" (v.13) mean for facing political or cultural upheaval today?
6. The psalm runs from "I" (v.1) to "all generations" (v.13). Who is downstream of your faith — whom are you actively teaching?
7. v.5 calls for meditation, not consumption. What would dwelling on one work of God for ten minutes look like this week?
