# Loved into Splendor

## A Group Study on Ephesians 5:22-33

> Wives, submit to your own husbands, as to the Lord.
> For the husband is the head of the wife even as Christ is the head of the church, his body, and is himself its Savior.
> Now as the church submits to Christ, so also wives should submit in everything to their husbands.
>
> Husbands, love your wives, as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her,
> that he might sanctify her, having cleansed her by the washing of water with the word,
> so that he might present the church to himself in splendor, without spot or wrinkle or any such thing, that she might be holy and without blemish.
> In the same way husbands should love their wives as their own bodies. He who loves his wife loves himself.
> For no one ever hated his own flesh, but nourishes and cherishes it, just as Christ does the church,
> because we are members of his body.
> "Therefore a man shall leave his father and mother and hold fast to his wife, and the two shall become one flesh."
> This mystery is profound, and I am saying that it refers to Christ and the church.
> However, let each one of you love his wife as himself, and let the wife see that she respects her husband.

---

### Introduction

A note about Paul: when he wrote this letter, he was under house arrest in Rome — a shut-in, chained to a guard, unable to go where he wanted or see the people he loved.
And Paul himself was single. He never married (1 Corinthians 7:7-8). Yet from that small room, this unmarried prisoner wrote the most profound vision of marriage in the entire Bible.

How? Because Paul understood that this passage is not ultimately about marriage advice.
It is about something bigger.
Every human marriage, Paul says, was designed from creation to be a portrait of Christ's love for His people.
That is the love story this passage tells.
Whether you are married, widowed, single, or separated — this love story includes you.
You are part of the Bride.

## Section 1: The Portrait

**Focus:** Ephesians 5:22-24 — *"Wives, submit to your own husbands, as to the Lord..."*

Ephesians 5:21 (for background)

> ...submitting to one another out of reverence for Christ.

### Context / Setup
- In the oldest manuscripts, v.22 has **no independent verb** — Paul's Greek flows straight from v.21
- Verses 21-22 read as a single thought: "submitting to one another out of reverence for Christ — wives, to your own husbands, as to the Lord"
- The wife's submission is a *specific instance* of the mutual submission commanded of every believer
- Paul devotes **three** verses to wives and **nine** to husbands — the weight falls on the one with power
- In a world where wifely deference was already the norm, Paul redirects far more teaching toward husbands

### Theological Anchor
- **Mutual submission** (v.21) is the frame for everything that follows
- Headship is defined by **rescue**, not rule — Paul bypasses "ruler/master/lord" and says "Savior"
- Whatever a husband's "headship" means, it is bounded by Christ-shaped self-sacrifice
- The wife's trust is ultimately directed to Christ — no husband can demand what Christ would not

### Language Notes
- **submit** (Greek: *hypotassō*) — originally a military term, "to arrange under"; Paul uses the middle voice → **voluntary self-ordering**
- Compliance = doing what you're told.  Submission = freely making another's will your own because you trust the one leading
- **as to the Lord** — four words that dignify and bound the command simultaneously
- **head** (Greek: *kephalē*) — here qualified by v.23's definition: "he is himself its *Savior*"

### Talking Points
- The grammar carries a theology: mutual submission frames everything
- Paul could have picked "ruler" or "master"; he deliberately chose "Savior"
- Most of Paul's ink goes to husbands — that is itself a rebuke of the ancient world's assumptions
- "Submission" is not "compliance" — one is self-chosen trust, the other is external pressure
- The wife's trust runs through her husband *to Christ* — she never submits to anything Christ himself would not command

### For Us
- This passage is about marriage, but the principle applies to every believer
- Every one of us — married or not — is called to trust Christ with things we cannot control
- Especially now.  Especially here.  When the body does not obey.  When the schedule is set by someone else.  When the future feels uncertain
- Submission to Christ means: *I trust You with my life — even the parts I can't manage anymore.  Even the parts that hurt*
- That is not weakness.  It is the deepest kind of strength

### Teaching Tip
The word "submit" is politically loud in our culture.  Do not fight that noise.  Simply show residents what Paul's Greek actually says: voluntary self-ordering, bounded by Christ, framed by mutuality.  The text defuses the caricature.

### Reflection Questions

1. Paul calls Christ "the Savior of the body" (v.23).  How has Christ been your Savior — not just at the cross, but in the daily realities of your life?

2. What is the difference between compliance (doing what you're told) and submission (making someone else's will your own)?  Can you think of a time you experienced that difference?

## Section 2: The Sacrifice

**Focus:** Ephesians 5:25-27 — *"Husbands, love your wives, as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her..."*

Ezekiel 16:8-10, 13-14 (for background)

> I bathed you with water and washed off your blood from you and anointed you with oil.
> I clothed you also with embroidered cloth and shod you with fine leather.
> I wrapped you in fine linen and covered you with silk...
> Your beauty was perfect through the splendor that I had bestowed on you, declares the Lord GOD.

### Context / Setup
- "Husbands, love your wives as Christ loved the church" — a command that shook the ancient world
- No first-century philosopher, rabbi, or Roman lawmaker had ever commanded husbands to love with self-sacrificial love
- Verses 25-27 are a single Greek sentence compressing the entire arc of salvation
- Paul echoes **Ezekiel 16** — God's image of rescuing, washing, clothing, and adorning his bride

### Theological Anchor
- The arc of salvation in three clauses:
  - **v.25** — *He gave himself up for her* → **atonement** (completed, historical)
  - **v.26** — *sanctify... cleansed by the washing of water with the word* → **sanctification** (ongoing)
  - **v.27** — *present the church to himself in splendor* → **glorification** (future)
- Christ does not wait for his Bride to clean herself up.  He bathes her
- "Without spot **or wrinkle**" — not only sin removed, but every mark of decay healed

### Language Notes
- **love** (Greek: *agapaō*) — present tense imperative; decision, not feeling; *keep on loving*
- **gave himself up** (Greek: *paredōken*) — same word used of the crucifixion; total self-expenditure
- **washing** (Greek: *loutron*) — a bath, not a rinse.  Echoes the bridal *mikveh* (Jewish ritual bath) and the Greek bride's sacred-spring washing
- **wrinkle** (Greek: *rhytida*) — appears only here in the entire NT; the creases of age and wear
- **in splendor** (Greek: *endoxon*) — radiant, glorious

### Talking Points
- The command that shook the ancient world: husbands, love like Christ did — with self-sacrifice
- One Greek sentence = the whole gospel: cross, sanctification, glorification
- Paul layers Jewish and Greek bridal imagery: the *mikveh*, the sacred-spring bath — Christ is doing both
- Ezekiel 16 stood in the background for 600 years — "I bathed you with water and washed off your blood" — now Paul reveals who was doing the washing all along
- Christ will present his Bride free from every spot of sin **and** every wrinkle of age
- Not hidden.  *Healed.*

### For Us
- Christ did not wait for us to become beautiful before he loved us — he loved us **into** beauty
- The One who loves you is not finished with you.  Still washing.  Still sanctifying.  Still preparing you
- Every ache, every limitation, every scar — he is working all of it toward glory
- **Christ does not love us because we are lovely.  He loves us until we are.**

### Teaching Tip
The "wrinkle" detail is gold for a room of aging believers.  Say it slowly.  Let it land.  Many have watched their face change in the mirror for decades — Christ promises to heal, not hide, every line.

### Reflection Questions

1. Christ promises to present His church "without spot or wrinkle."  What does that promise mean to you at this stage of life?

2. The word for "washing" means a full bath — not a quick rinse. What areas of your heart do you wish Christ would wash clean?

## Section 3: The Mystery

**Focus:** Ephesians 5:28-32 — *"This mystery is profound, and I am saying that it refers to Christ and the church."*

Genesis 2:23-24 (for background)

> Then the man said, "This at last is bone of my bones and flesh of my flesh;
> she shall be called Woman, because she was taken out of Man."
> Therefore a man shall leave his father and mother and hold fast to his wife,
> and they shall become one flesh.

### Context / Setup
- Paul reaches for the **oldest verse about marriage in the Bible** — Genesis 2:24
- He cites it to reveal something hidden since Eden
- Marriage was never just about two people.  It was always an icon
- Paul himself never married — yet he writes the most profound theology of marriage in the Bible

### Theological Anchor
- **Mystery** (Greek: *mystērion*) = not a puzzle, but a secret hidden in God's plan, now revealed
- From Eden onward, one-flesh marriage has been a **living portrait** of Christ's union with the church
- The great reveal: "This mystery is profound — it refers to Christ and the church"
- We can finally see what was always there

### Language Notes
- **nourishes** (Greek: *ektrephō*) — feeds to maturity.  Same word Paul uses in Eph 6:4 for fathers raising children
- **cherishes** (Greek: *thalpō*) — warms, broods over.  Used of a nursing mother warming a child against her body (1 Thess 2:7)
- **hold fast** (Greek: *proskollaō*) — literally "to be glued to"; a bond so strong it cannot be dissolved without destroying what was joined.  Same word as Ruth clinging to Naomi (Ruth 1:14); same as Israel commanded to cling to God (Deut 10:20)

### Talking Points
- Two remarkably tender words: *nourishes* (feeds to maturity) and *cherishes* (warms, broods over).  Not words of authority — words of tenderness
- Christ does not rule his people from a distance.  He feeds them and holds them close
- Genesis 2:24 was always prophecy as much as description
- The incarnation is Christ "leaving his father's house." The cross is Christ "holding fast" to his bride.  Eternity is Christ "never letting go"
- You do not need to be currently married to stand in awe of this mystery — you need only to be part of the Bride

### For Us
- Some of you had beautiful marriages.  Some had difficult ones.  Some never married.  Some have lost the one you loved most
- The earthly marriage was always a **shadow** of something greater
- Christ left his Father's house — that is what the incarnation was
- Christ "held fast" to his Bride — that is what the cross was
- Christ will never let go — that is what eternity is
- You are nourished.  You are cherished.  You are held fast.  Not by a human spouse who may be gone now, but by Christ, who said: *I will never leave you nor forsake you* (Heb 13:5)

### Teaching Tip
This is where widows, widowers, and the never-married most need the passage to reach them.  Do not make the earthly marriage the main event — Paul doesn't.  The earthly marriage is the icon; Christ and the church are the reality.

### Reflection Questions

1. Paul says Christ "nourishes and cherishes" the church — using words for feeding and warming. In what ways has Christ nourished or warmed you recently?

2. "This mystery is profound." What is one thing about God's love that still amazes you, even after all these years of knowing Him?

## Section 4: The Ending That Is a Beginning

**Focus:** Ephesians 5:33 — *"However, let each one of you love his wife as himself, and let the wife see that she respects her husband."*

Revelation 19:7, 9

> Let us rejoice and exult and give him the glory,
> for the marriage of the Lamb has come, and his Bride has made herself ready...
> Blessed are those who are invited to the marriage supper of the Lamb.

### Context / Setup
- V.33 begins with *plēn* ("however / nevertheless") — descending from christological heights to the runway
- After all that theology, Paul lands with a practical sentence for Monday morning
- The passage opens and closes with the same word-family: **reverence / respect** (*phobos* / *phobeō*)
- And the larger story does not stop here — it runs to Revelation 19

### Theological Anchor
- The whole Bible is one love story, Genesis to Revelation
- Marriage is the opening image (Gen 2) and the closing image (Rev 19-21)
- **"Blessed are those who are invited"** — not "blessed are the bride and groom." The invitation is the gift
- That invitation does not expire with age, health, or mobility.  It requires only a *yes*

### Language Notes
- **respect** (Greek: *phobeō*) — sometimes "fear," here deep reverence.  Same family as v.21's "reverence (*phobos*) for Christ" — the passage begins and ends with the same word
- **bride has made herself ready** (Rev 19:7) — the Bride's readiness is itself a gift; she did not manufacture her own splendor

### Talking Points
- Paul's bookends: reverence for Christ at the open, respect within marriage at the close.  Reverence for Christ flows into every relationship
- The Bible's love story:
  - **Genesis 2** — God creates the first marriage
  - **Hosea** — God as a faithful husband to an unfaithful people
  - **Isaiah 54** — "Your Maker is your husband"
  - **Ezekiel 16** — God washes, clothes, adorns his bride
  - **Song of Solomon** — the Lover and the Beloved
  - **Ephesians 5** — the great reveal
  - **Revelation 19** — "The marriage of the Lamb has come"
  - **Revelation 21** — the Bible ends where it began, with a marriage
- The invitation is the key word.  "Blessed are those who are *invited*"

### For Us
- For many of us, the earthly chapter of marriage is closed — a spouse passed, a ring in a drawer, the shared house in someone else's name
- But the great chapter is still ahead
- The Bible does not end with a funeral.  It ends with a wedding feast
- Whatever you have lost — spouse, health, independence, home — the best is not behind you.  The best is ahead
- The Lamb's supper is prepared.  The Bride is being made ready.  And you — sitting in this room, in this chair, in this body — you are part of that Bride
- One day every *spot* of sin and every *wrinkle* of age will be gone.  Not because you earned it, but because He loved you into splendor

### Teaching Tip
End quietly.  This is the section that lingers longest with residents.  Do not rush the close.  Let the image of the wedding feast settle over the room like light.

### Reflection Questions

1. The Bible begins with a marriage (Genesis 2) and ends with a marriage (Revelation 19).  What does it mean to you that God chose a wedding as the final image of eternity?

2. Paul lands from the heights of theology with a very practical verse: love, respect. What is one practical way you can show love or respect to someone this week — a roommate, a caretaker, a family member?

---

### For Reflection

This week, each morning when you wake up — before your feet touch the floor (or before you reach for the call button) — say these words quietly:

*"I am loved.  I am being made ready.  The best is still ahead."*

That is what Ephesians 5 says about you.

- **If you are a widow or widower:** thank God for the marriage you had, even with its imperfections.  It was a shadow of something greater.  And that greater love has not ended — it has only begun
- **If you never married:** you have never been without a Bridegroom.  Christ has held fast to you your whole life
- **If your marriage was painful:** Christ knows.  He married a bride who was unfaithful too (Hosea 1-3).  And He never stopped loving her.  He never stopped washing.  He never stopped preparing her for splendor

This week, ask one person — a roommate, an aide, a visitor — *"How can I pray for you?"* You may be the only person who asks them that today.  A member of the Bride who intercedes for others is doing the most important work in the building.

The passage began with a portrait of trust (v.22) and ended with a wedding feast (Revelation 19:7).  Between those bookends is the whole gospel: Christ loved, Christ sacrificed, Christ washes, Christ presents in splendor.  And you — right where you are — are part of that story.  None of it is wasted.  All of it matters.  The Bridegroom is coming.  And He is not ashamed of His Bride.

### Passage Structure at a Glance

```
THE PORTRAIT (vv.22-24)
  → Wives: voluntary trust, "as to the Lord"
  → The husband as head — defined by: "he is the Savior"

THE SACRIFICE (vv.25-27)
  → Husbands: love as Christ loved — gave himself up
  → Christ's threefold work:
       gave himself    (ATONEMENT)
       sanctifies      (SANCTIFICATION)
       presents in splendor  (GLORIFICATION)

THE MYSTERY (vv.28-32)
  → "No one ever hated his own flesh"
  → Nourishes and cherishes — as Christ does the church
  → Genesis 2:24: "the two shall become one flesh"
  → THE GREAT REVEAL: "This mystery is profound —
       it refers to Christ and the church"

THE LANDING (v.33)
  → Husband: love your wife as yourself
  → Wife: respect your husband
  → From cosmic mystery back to daily practice
  → But the story does not end here — Revelation 19:7
```
