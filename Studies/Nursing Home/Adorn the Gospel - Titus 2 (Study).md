# Adorn the Gospel

## A Group Study on Titus 2:1-10

> But as for you, teach what accords with sound doctrine.
> Older men are to be sober-minded, dignified, self-controlled, sound in faith, in love, and in steadfastness.
> Older women likewise are to be reverent in behavior, not slanderers or slaves to much wine. They are to teach what is good,
> and so train the young women to love their husbands and children,
> to be self-controlled, pure, working at home, kind, and submissive to their own husbands, that the word of God may not be reviled.
> Likewise, urge the younger men to be self-controlled.
> Show yourself in all respects to be a model of good works, and in your teaching show integrity, dignity,
> and sound speech that cannot be condemned, so that an opponent may be put to shame, having nothing evil to say about us.
> Slaves are to be submissive to their own masters in everything; they are to be well-pleasing, not argumentative,
> not pilfering, but showing all good faith, so that in everything they may adorn the doctrine of God our Savior.
> *(Titus 2:1-10, ESV)*

---

### Setting the Scene

  When Paul wrote these words, he was an old man near the end of his ministry — somewhere between his first and second Roman imprisonments (about AD 63–66).  He had stopped on the island of Crete with his younger colleague Titus, planted small churches in several Cretan towns, and then sailed on — leaving Titus behind to "put what remained into order" (1:5).

  Crete was a difficult place.  Even the Cretans' own poet had said so (1:12).  The new believers lived in extended households of free people and slaves, often a single Christian convert surrounded by family members who were not.  Paul has just finished chapter 1, where he lashed the false teachers who were unsettling whole households (1:11).

Now, in chapter 2, the tone changes.  Paul turns from fixing the troublemakers to building up the saints.

And note who he addresses **first**.

Not Titus's peers.  Not the young leaders.  Not the new converts.

He addresses the **older men**.  Then the **older women**.  Then — through the older women — the young wives and mothers.  Then the young men.  Then Titus himself.  Then the slaves.

The gray-haired sit nearest the front of Paul's instructional line.  They are the ones who set the tone for everything that follows.

That is a letter for this room.

---

## 1. Sound Doctrine Looks Like Something (v.1)

**Focus:** *"But as for you, teach what accords with sound doctrine."* (v.1)

### Context / Setup
- "But as for you" is emphatic in Greek — Paul is drawing a hard line between Titus and the false teachers of 1:10-16
- Titus's job is not to invent doctrine but to **teach** what accords with the sound apostolic teaching he has already received
- The word "sound" runs all through this letter (1:9, 13; 2:1, 2, 8) — it is a controlling metaphor

### Theological Anchor
- "Sound" (*hygiainō*) means **healthy**.  The same root gives us our English word *hygiene*
- Sick teaching produces sick souls; healthy teaching produces healthy lives
- The next nine verses will show what "healthy" looks like in different stations — older men, older women, younger women, younger men, slaves
- Doctrine and life are inseparable.  Teaching that leaves a life unchanged has been heard but not yet received.

### Language Notes
- **Sound** (Greek: ὑγιαίνω, *hygiainō*) — to be healthy.  A Hippocratic medical term Paul borrows for doctrine
- **Doctrine** (Greek: διδασκαλία, *didaskalia*) — both the act of teaching and the content taught
- **Accords with** (Greek: πρέπει, *prepei*) — befits, is fitting.  Sound doctrine has a *shape* it produces in life

### Talking Points
- Paul does not ask Titus to be original.  He asks him to be **faithful** to the pattern
- The medical metaphor is striking: a doctrine that does not produce health is, by Paul's measure, not sound
- Notice the contrast with chapter 1.  False teachers (1:10-16) are described as "unfit for any good work" (1:16).  Sound doctrine is fit for *every* good work
- Paul will not separate "what you believe" from "how you live."  In his vocabulary they are the same word — *sound*

### For Us
- After a long life of believing, the deepest proof of doctrine is not what you can recite — it is what is left in your bones when most of the recitation is gone
- "Sound" in a nursing home looks like: still trusting when the body fails; still kind when the aide is rough; still patient when memory slips; still hopeful when friends pass
- Many in this room have absorbed decades of teaching.  The question Paul presses is whether that teaching has become *flesh*
- A doctrine you can no longer argue is not lost.  If it has shaped your character, it is *more* real now than it was when you could explain it

## 2. Older Saints Set the Tone (vv.2-3)

**Focus:** *"Older men are to be sober-minded, dignified, self-controlled, sound in faith, in love, and in steadfastness.  Older women likewise are to be reverent in behavior, not slanderers or slaves to much wine.  They are to teach what is good."* (vv.2-3)

### Context / Setup
- Paul addresses **older men** first — they go to the front of the line
- "Older men" here (*presbytas*) is a demographic, not a church office — senior men in the congregation, not "elders" in the technical sense
- He lists **five** marks of a faithful older man: sober-minded, dignified, self-controlled, sound in faith, sound in love, sound in steadfastness
- Then he turns to the **older women** with a three-part profile and a teaching mandate

### Theological Anchor
- The virtues Paul names are not new acquisitions — they are the **long-obedience-deepening** of habits formed over decades
- **Steadfastness** (*hypomonē*) is the older man's signature virtue.  It does not mean stoic endurance — it means actively staying put under load
- "Sound in faith, in love, and in steadfastness" reshapes Paul's familiar triad (faith, hope, love).  For older saints, hope has matured into endurance
- "Reverent in behavior" (*hieroprepēs*) applies temple-language to ordinary daily bearing.  The older Christian woman's daily walk is **fit for sacred things**
- "Slanderers" — the Greek word is *diabolos*, **the same word as "devil."** Paul is not being cute.  Gossip is devilish work
- "Slaves to much wine" — Paul knew his world.  Roman matrons had ready access to household wine stores; alcoholism among older women was a known social ill

### Language Notes
- **Older men** (Greek: πρεσβύτας, *presbytas*) — senior men, demographically
- **Sober-minded** (Greek: νηφάλιος, *nēphalios*) — clear-headed, especially with wine; also morally steady
- **Dignified** (Greek: σεμνός, *semnos*) — weighty, worthy of respect; the Roman idea of *gravitas*
- **Self-controlled** (Greek: σώφρων, *sōphrōn*) — a cardinal Greek virtue; "sound-minded."  Watch this word — it appears **four times** in six verses
- **Steadfastness** (Greek: ὑπομονή, *hypomonē*) — active perseverance under pressure
- **Older women** (Greek: πρεσβύτιδας, *presbytidas*) — senior women, demographically
- **Reverent in behavior** (Greek: ἱεροπρεπής, *hieroprepēs*) — "fitting for sacred things."  A rare and elevated word
- **Slanderers** (Greek: διάβολοι, *diaboloi*) — same word translated "devil" elsewhere
- **Enslaved to wine** (Greek: δεδουλωμένας οἴνῳ, *dedoulōmenas oinō*) — a perfect-tense passive: "having been enslaved" by wine.  Notice the slavery imagery — it will return in v.9
- **Teachers of good** (Greek: καλοδιδάσκαλοι, *kalodidaskaloi*) — a rare word, possibly coined by Paul, naming the older woman's teaching ministry

### Talking Points
- Paul addresses older men *first*.  Not because they are loudest, but because they are seasoned.  In the church, the gray-haired set the moral tone
- The five virtues for older men describe what *long* obedience looks like — not what *new* obedience promises.  These are the ripening fruits of decades, not the starter kit
- *Hypomonē* — staying put under load — is the older man's signature gift.  The finishing virtue
- "Reverent in behavior" — Paul applies priestly vocabulary to ordinary daily bearing.  The older Christian woman's ordinary walk carries a sacred weight
- Paul does not say older women *might* teach.  He gives them a name — *kalodidaskaloi*, teachers of good — and an assignment
- "Slanderers" / *diabolous* — vivid and unsparing.  Paul will not pretend gossip is merely a personality quirk

### For Us
- The dignity of long obedience belongs to **this room**.  Paul put your demographic at the front of the line for a reason
- For older men: *hypomonē* is your finishing virtue.  Stay put under load.  Do not drift in the last mile
- For older women: you are *kalodidaskaloi* — teachers of good — by Paul's own word.  You do not need a classroom; your daily bearing already teaches
- Speech-watch.  Communal settings — including nursing homes — run on talk.  Refuse the devilish kind, even when it is dressed up as a prayer request or a concern
- Substance-watch.  Wine, food, prescription drugs, screens — anything that *enslaves* is dangerous at any age
- "Fit for sacred things" — your bedside, your chair, your hallway is a sacred space when a Christian woman or man inhabits it well

## 3. Pass It On (vv.4-6)

**Focus:** *"...so train the young women to love their husbands and children, to be self-controlled, pure, working at home, kind, and submissive to their own husbands, that the word of God may not be reviled. Likewise, urge the younger men to be self-controlled."* (vv.4-6)

### Context / Setup
- Note who Paul does **not** send to teach the young wives.  He does not send Titus.  He routes the instruction *through the older women*
- Paul knows what every seasoned saint knows: peer-elder teaching is more durable than instruction from an outside expert
- The young wives are trained in **seven** virtues; the young men receive **one** — self-control
- Why only one virtue for young men? Because *sōphrōn* is the gate behind which all the others stand

### Theological Anchor
- Discipleship is **intergenerational and obligatory**, not optional or one-directional
- The home is the first seminary; the older Christian woman is its faculty
- The connective virtue across **every age and gender** Paul addresses is *sōphrōn* — self-control.  He uses the *sōphrōn* word-family **four times in six verses** (vv.2, 4, 5, 6).  That is no accident
- The witness motive surfaces explicitly in v.5: *"that the word of God may not be reviled."* How young Christian families live is part of the gospel's public reputation

### Language Notes
- **Train** (Greek: σωφρονίζω, *sōphronizō*) — the causative verb: older women *make* younger women *sōphrōn*.  Hands-on, life-on-life shaping
- **Lovers of husbands** (Greek: φίλανδροι, *philandroi*) — "husband-lovers"
- **Lovers of children** (Greek: φιλότεκνοι, *philoteknoi*) — "child-lovers"
- **Pure** (Greek: ἁγνός, *hagnos*) — chaste, morally clean
- **Working at home** (Greek: οἰκουργοί, *oikourgoi*) — the household-worker.  Not idle keepers but productive stewards of the home as economic unit
- **Submissive** (Greek: ὑποτασσομένας, *hypotassomenas*) — voluntary middle voice; "placing themselves under."  Not coerced obedience but ordered self-placement
- **Younger men** (Greek: νεώτεροι, *neōteroi*) — adult men in their prime
- **Urge** (Greek: παρακάλει, *parakalei*) — exhort, encourage, come alongside
- **Self-controlled** (Greek: σωφρονεῖν, *sōphronein*) — the same *sōphrōn* family, now applied to young men

### Talking Points
- Older women's "training" is not formal lecture but *life-on-life* — the kind of teaching that happens in the kitchen, on a walk, across a meal table
- Seven virtues for young wives, one for young men — but the *same* underlying virtue (*sōphrōn*) holds them all together
- Paul does not flatter the young women into thinking they already have it.  He gives them concrete things to receive
- The witness clause at v.5b — *"that the word of God may not be reviled"* — is the first of two purpose statements that bracket this whole section (the second is at v.10b).  Christian household life is missionary

### For Us
- The intergenerational discipling chain depends on **you**.  You may not see the immediate fruit, but you don't need to.  You are not the harvester
- For older women: think of one younger woman in your life — daughter, granddaughter, niece, nurse, neighbor, fellow resident — and one piece of marriage / mothering / household / faith wisdom you have lived long enough to know.  *That* is your *kalodidaskalos* work
- For older men: think of one younger man in your life — son, grandson, son-in-law, an aide — and what *sōphrōn* (self-control) cost you when it cost you something
- Tell stories.  Stories are training.  Paul's whole plan here is that older saints train younger ones through life-on-life conversation
- The seeds you plant now may not sprout for twenty years.  Plant them anyway.  Lois did not live to see Timothy become a pastor

## 4. Adorn the Doctrine (vv.7-10)

**Focus:** *"Show yourself in all respects to be a model of good works... so that in everything they may adorn the doctrine of God our Savior."* (vv.7, 10)

### Context / Setup
- Paul pivots to Titus himself — the teacher must embody the teaching
- "Model" (*typos*) is the stamp-and-impression word.  Titus's life is the seal that imprints the next generation
- Then Paul turns to the **slaves** — the lowest-status persons in the household — and gives **them** the climactic instruction
- v.10 is the chapter's high note: *"that they may **adorn** the doctrine of God our Savior."*

### Theological Anchor
- Teaching must be embodied.  Words plus matching life equal credibility; words without matching life equal noise
- **Adorn** (*kosmeō*) is the root of our English word *cosmetic*.  Beautify.  Make attractive
- The one Paul gives the adornment task to is **the slave** — the most legally diminished person in the household
- The inversion is theologically pointed: the lowest-status person *beautifies* the highest reality (the doctrine of God our Savior)
- This means: **every station, however lowly socially, can make the gospel beautiful**
- The principle transfers: the patient under nursing care, the elderly believer dependent on staff and family, the resident among aides and nurses — every one of them is positioned to adorn the doctrine

### Language Notes
- **Model** (Greek: τύπος, *typos*) — a stamp, an impression, a pattern.  Titus's life is the seal that presses into others
- **Integrity** (Greek: ἀφθορία, *aphthoria*) — incorruption; not adulterated
- **Dignity** (Greek: σεμνότης, *semnotēs*) — the noun form of the *semnos* applied to older men in v.2
- **Sound speech** (Greek: λόγον ὑγιῆ, *logon hygiē*) — *healthy* speech; the same *hygiē* root from v.1
- **Cannot be condemned** (Greek: ἀκατάγνωστον, *akatagnōston*) — beyond reproach; nothing critics can legitimately attack
- **Slaves** (Greek: δούλους, *doulous*) — same root Paul uses of himself in 1:1 ("a *servant* of God")
- **Masters** (Greek: δεσπόταις, *despotais*) — absolute lords; stronger than the more common *kyrios*
- **Well-pleasing** (Greek: εὐαρέστους, *euarestous*) — aiming to please
- **Argumentative** (Greek: ἀντιλέγοντας, *antilegontas*) — "speaking against"; back-talk
- **Pilfering** (Greek: νοσφιζομένους, *nosphizomenous*) — the same word used of Ananias and Sapphira embezzling from the Acts 5 offering
- **Adorn** (Greek: κοσμέω, *kosmeō*) — beautify, decorate.  The root of our English word *cosmetic*

### Talking Points
- *Typos* — the stamp.  Titus's life is the impression that imprints others.  This is why pastors and elders cannot afford a gap between word and life
- "Sound speech that cannot be condemned" — Paul wants Titus's words to be invulnerable to critics not because he is clever but because he is honest
- Paul could have skipped the slaves; the surrounding culture certainly did.  Instead he addresses them **directly** as full moral agents whose conduct can *adorn* the gospel.  This was theologically and socially radical
- Notice the slave's "weapons" — they are not big: not argumentative, not pilfering, showing good faith.  Small, hidden, daily things — the kind a master, a nurse, an aide sees
- The two purpose clauses now show clearly: *"that the word of God may not be reviled"* (v.5) and *"that they may adorn the doctrine of God our Savior"* (v.10).  Christian living is missionary witness, top to bottom

### For Us
- Paul's claim — and it is breathtaking — is that **the most diminished person in this room can adorn the gospel**
- This is not about platforms.  It is about daily faithfulness that makes Jesus look like he is worth following
- Concretely, what does "adorning" look like in a nursing home? — a thank-you to the aide who is having a hard day; refusing to badmouth the staff; not pilfering little advantages or attention or extra portions; receiving care with grace; greeting the next person in the hallway by name; speaking sound, healthy words instead of complaint
- "Not argumentative" (v.9) is a startling commandment in a place where so much can go wrong and so little is in your control.  Paul still says it
- Your faithful smallness in *this* season may be the only sermon some watching person ever sees.  The aide, the night nurse, the visiting grandchild — they are reading you
- The gospel does not need your strength.  It needs your faithfulness.  *Kosmeō* is well within reach for any believer at any stage of life

## Review & Reflection

These questions draw from all four sections.  Pick the ones that fit your group.  Leave silences — some of the best answers come slowly.

1. "Sound doctrine" (v.1) is *healthy* doctrine.  If a stranger watched you this week, what kind of doctrine would they say is "healthy" in your life?

2. Paul lists five marks of a faithful older man in v.2: sober-minded, dignified, self-controlled, sound in faith, in love, in steadfastness.  Which of those is most alive in you right now?  Which one has gone quietest?

3. v.3 says older women are to be *reverent in behavior* — fit for sacred things, even in ordinary daily life.  What in your daily bearing this week would qualify as "fit for sacred things"?

4. Paul calls slander "devilish" (*diabolos* in v.3 is literally "devil").  Where in your speech — phone calls, hallway conversations, even prayer requests — is there gossip dressed up as concern?

5. Older women are *teachers of good* (*kalodidaskaloi*, v.3).  Who is one younger person you are positioned to teach right now — even informally, through stories, prayers, or one piece of long-tested wisdom?

6. Self-control (*sōphrōn*) appears four times in six verses.  Paul thinks it matters at every age.  Where in your life today does *sōphrōn* — a sound, ordered mind — need to grow?

7. Titus is told to be a *typos* — a stamp, a model (v.7).  Whose life is currently stamping yours?  Whose life are you currently stamping?

8. Twice Paul names the witness purpose: *"that the word of God may not be reviled"* (v.5) and *"that they may adorn the doctrine"* (v.10).  When was the last time you weighed something you said or did by that scale?

9. The slave — the lowest-status person in the household — is the one who *adorns* the doctrine of God our Savior (v.10).  What is one small, hidden faithfulness in your day-to-day life right now that beautifies the gospel?

10. If everything else were stripped away — strength, mobility, memory, position — would you still believe what Paul calls *sound doctrine*?  What part of it would be left?

### This Week — Two Practices

- **One small adornment.** Choose one daily act — a thank-you to an aide, a smile across the dining room, refusing one piece of gossip, a kind word to a roommate — and do it every day this week.  *Kosmeō.* Make the gospel beautiful in your one small corner

- **One Lois move.** Pick one younger person — a child, grandchild, niece, nephew, nurse, visitor — and tell them one thing the Lord has taught you through a long obedience.  A phone call, a card, a sentence at the meal table.  You are a *kalodidaskalos* (teacher of good) by Paul's own word

The Lord who has been training you this long is still training you.  The doctrine of God our Savior is still beautiful — and you are still positioned to adorn it.
