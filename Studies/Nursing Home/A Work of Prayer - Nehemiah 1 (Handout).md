# A Work of Prayer

## A Group Study on Nehemiah 1:1-11

> And they said to me, "The remnant there... is in great trouble and shame. The wall of Jerusalem is broken down, and its gates are destroyed by fire."
> As soon as I heard these words I sat down and wept and mourned for days, and I continued fasting and praying before the God of heaven.
> And I said, "O LORD God of heaven, the great and awesome God who keeps covenant and steadfast love..."
> Now I was cupbearer to the king.
> *(Selected from Nehemiah 1:3-11)*

*A note about Nehemiah:* He lived in Susa, the Persian capital, 800 miles away from Jerusalem. He had a secure job serving the king, but his heart was with his people. When he heard their city walls were ruined, he felt what many of us feel: the ache of being far away from loved ones when they are hurting. But Nehemiah shows us that the greatest work often begins in a chair, with a prayer.

---

## 1. The Posture of Prayer (vv.3-4)

The news from Jerusalem was dangerous—without walls, the people were defenseless. Nehemiah’s immediate reaction was to sit down and weep. For a high-ranking official, this was a profound disruption, but he turned his grief straight into prayer. 

**For us:** Sitting down is a posture many of us know well. Sometimes it comes with a feeling of limitation or being sidelined. But in this story, sitting is where the real work begins. If you are sitting in a chair today, grieving the struggles of your family, you are in the exact same posture as Nehemiah. Your room can become a place of powerful intercession.

### Questions
1. Have you ever received bad news and felt the frustration of being too far away or physically unable to help? 
2. How does it change our perspective to view sitting and praying as an active, vital work rather than just "doing nothing"?

---

## 2. Remembering Who God Is (v.5)

Before Nehemiah asked God for anything, he declared who God is: the "God of heaven," the "great and awesome God who keeps covenant and steadfast love." He placed the brokenness of his people right up against the unbreakable love of God. 

**For us:** When we worry about people we love, our problems seem massive and our God can seem small. Nehemiah teaches us to look past the "ruined walls" and fix our eyes on God. When you pray for your children or neighbors, you appeal to a God whose steadfast, never-giving-up love never ceases. 

### Questions
1. When you face a crisis, what helps you focus on the character of God rather than just the size of the problem?
2. What does it mean to you today that God's love is a "steadfast," never-giving-up kind of love?

---

## 3. Right Where You Are (v.11)

At the end of his prayer, Nehemiah drops a casual detail: "Now I was cupbearer to the king." He wasn't a priest or prophet; he had a secular job. But his access to the king was exactly what God would use to rebuild Jerusalem. 

**For us:** You might think, "I'm just a resident here. What can I do?" But God has you exactly where you are for a purpose. You cross paths with nurses, aides, and other residents who carry heavy burdens. You have time to pray. "Now I was a resident in this home" is your title, and it is a strategic placement by the God of heaven.

### Questions
1. How can God use your specific situation right now to bless others?
2. Is there someone in this facility—staff or resident—who needs the encouragement you can uniquely provide this week?

---

### For Reflection

This week, try two things:

**First:** Turn your sitting into intercession. Pick one person—a family member far away, or a staff member right down the hall—and bring them before the God who keeps His promises.

**Second:** Remind yourself that you are strategically placed. Smile at the person delivering your meals. Ask an aide how their family is doing. The work of rebuilding begins with one faithful person exactly where God put them.
