# Take Up Your Cross

## A Group Study on Mark 8:31-9:1

> And he began to teach them that the Son of Man must suffer many things and be rejected by the elders and the chief priests and the scribes and be killed, and after three days rise again.
> And he said this plainly.  And Peter took him aside and began to rebuke him.
> But turning and seeing his disciples, he rebuked Peter and said, "Get behind me, Satan! For you are not setting your mind on the things of God, but on the things of man."
> And calling the crowd to him with his disciples, he said to them, "If anyone would come after me, let him deny himself and take up his cross and follow me.
> For whoever would save his life will lose it, but whoever loses his life for my sake and the gospel's will save it.
> For what does it profit a man to gain the whole world and forfeit his soul?
> For what can a man give in return for his soul?
> For whoever is ashamed of me and of my words in this adulterous and sinful generation, of him will the Son of Man also be ashamed when he comes in the glory of his Father with the holy angels."
> And he said to them, "Truly, I say to you, there are some standing here who will not taste death until they see the kingdom of God after it has come in power."

---

### Introduction

Mark wrote this Gospel for Christians in Rome during the reign of Nero.  The emperor had just begun blaming believers for the Great Fire of AD 64.  Christians were being crucified in public gardens.  Some were being wrapped in animal skins and torn apart by dogs.  Some were being burned alive as torches to light Nero's parties.  When Mark put these words of Jesus down on parchment — *take up your cross and follow me* — he was not handing out a spiritual metaphor.  He was handing his readers a description of what their Monday morning might look like.

That same Jesus speaks to us.  Most of us will not be crucified.  But the cross has a thousand shapes, and Jesus has a way of meeting people on whatever road they are actually walking.

For many of you, the cross you carry was not chosen.  A body that used to work and doesn't.  A husband or wife gone ahead.  A child who doesn't visit.  A name the nurse keeps getting wrong.  A world that walks past you faster every year.  You did not sign up for any of it — and yet here you are, still here, still hoping.

This passage is for people on that road.  Jesus does not promise to take the cross away.  He does something better.  He walks ahead, he turns around, and he says: *Come behind me.  I know this road.  I have walked it myself.*

By the end, he promises that some of those standing there will see the Kingdom of God come in power.  They did — and so will you.

## Section 1: The Necessary Road (vv.31-33)

**Focus:** Mark 8:31-33 — *"And he began to teach them that the Son of Man must suffer many things… And Peter took him aside and began to rebuke him."*

### Context / Setup
- First "passion prediction" in Mark — a hinge point in the Gospel
- Seven chapters of miracles, healings, parables; now Jesus turns to the cross
- Jesus spoke "plainly" (*parrēsia*) — no more parables about his death
- Peter pulls Jesus aside physically — *manages* him, tries to lead from the front

### Theological Anchor
- The cross is not accident but **divine necessity** — the Father's plan from the beginning
- Resurrection is predicted *in the same breath* as the crucifixion — both halves belong together
- The three groups who reject him (elders, chief priests, scribes) = the Sanhedrin, Israel's most respected religious men
- Satan's wilderness temptation reappears through Peter's well-meaning mouth: *a kingdom without a cross*

### Language Notes
- **must** (Greek: *dei*) — "it is necessary"; the small word carrying divine necessity.  Not "may" or "might"
- **plainly** (Greek: *parrēsia*) — openly, frankly; the end of parable-speech about his death
- **Get behind me** (Greek: *hupage opisō mou*) — literally "go behind me"; the exact phrase from Mark 1:17's call to discipleship.  Repositioning, not dismissal
- **Satan** — Hebrew for "adversary"; Peter is not the devil, but the devil's line comes out of his mouth (cf. Matt 4:8-10)
- **setting your mind on** (Greek: *phroneō*) — orientation, allegiance, not mere thinking

### Talking Points
- "Must" is the heaviest word in the sentence — the cross is plan, not tragedy
- Peter tries to spare Jesus; Jesus calls the sparing voice Satanic
- Note the reversal: the disciple who steps in front of Jesus to protect him is actually blocking him
- "Get behind me" is not banishment — it's the same phrase as "come after me." Peter is being put *back in formation*
- The disciples heard "be killed" and stopped listening; they missed "rise again"

### For Us
- People who love you will try to talk you out of the road God has you on — and they mean well
- "Stop praying so much." "Don't talk about death." "Church is too much for you now." Some of that is love.  Some is Peter
- The cross of aging, loss, dependence — Jesus has walked this road.  He does not ask you to walk where he has not gone
- Your job: keep your face toward the road, even when kind voices urge you off it

### Teaching Tip
Watch for the moment someone in the room recognizes a "Peter voice" from their own life — a loving person whose care became a barrier.  This passage is permission to honor the love *and* keep walking behind Jesus.

### Reflection Questions

1.  Has anyone, out of love, tried to talk you out of something you knew God was asking of you? How did you tell the difference between their care and God's call?

2.  Jesus predicted his death and his resurrection in the same breath, but the disciples only heard the first half.  When you think about the road ahead, how easy or hard is it to remember both the cross *and* the rising?

## Section 2: Deny, Take Up, Follow (vv.34-37)

**Focus:** Mark 8:34-37 — *"If anyone would come after me, let him deny himself and take up his cross and follow me."*

### Context / Setup
- Mark's camera pulls back: Jesus calls "**the crowd**" — cross-bearing is public, not insider teaching
- Three verbs in careful order: **deny → take up → follow**
- First two verbs are aorist (decisive, once-for-all); the third is present tense (continuous, daily)
- Then the paradox (save/lose) and the marketplace image (soul vs.  the world)

### Theological Anchor
- Discipleship is **self-displacement**, not self-improvement — taking the crown off your own head
- The cross is not ornament; it is the execution beam.  "Take up your cross" = "start walking toward your own death"
- The *psychē* pun: you can save your biological life and lose your true self; or spend your life for Christ and find both
- A soul has no marketplace equivalent — only God at the cross pays what a soul is worth

### Language Notes
- **deny himself** (Greek: *aparneomai*) — to disown, renounce; the exact word Peter uses three times in Mark 14:30, 72
- **cross** (Greek: *stauros*) — the Roman execution beam; the condemned carried the crossbeam (*patibulum*, 75-125 lbs) through jeering crowds
- **follow** — present tense imperative: ongoing, day after day
- **life/soul** (Greek: *psychē*) — both physical life *and* true self; Jesus uses the pun deliberately
- **give in exchange** (Greek: *antallagma*) — marketplace term, "price in a trade"

### Talking Points
- Three verbs, in order.  Order matters: you cannot take up a cross while you are still on the throne
- Deny yourself now — or you will deny Christ later (Peter's three denials use the same verb)
- First-century hearers felt the horror: "take up your cross" was no metaphor
- We've turned the cross into jewelry; pull the word back to its original weight
- "Follow" is present tense — you do not graduate from following.  Tomorrow.  The next day.  The next
- The soul is priceless — put the entire world on one side of the scale, a soul on the other, and the soul tips it

### For Us
- The cross you did not choose — the body, the memory, the empty chair — you are already carrying
- The question is not *whether* you will carry it.  The question is *whether you will carry it behind him*
- Self-pity carries the cross alone; faith carries it in Jesus' footsteps
- You have watched a whole generation trade the world for their souls and lose both.  Your silent prayer in this chair is worth more than a skyscraper with your name on it
- You know, better than anyone younger, that Jesus weighed the scales right

### Teaching Tip
This is the passage where residents most often weep.  The Lord is speaking directly to a cross they carry every day.  Give space.  Do not rush the application.

### Reflection Questions

1.  Jesus tells us to take up a cross.  Many of you are already carrying one that you did not choose.  What would it look like to carry it *with* Jesus rather than alone?

2.  You have watched a whole generation chase after "the world."  What have you come to understand about the worth of a soul that a younger person might not yet see?

## Section 3: Not Ashamed, Kingdom Come (v.38-9:1)

**Focus:** Mark 8:38-9:1 — *"Whoever is ashamed of me… of him will the Son of Man also be ashamed when he comes in the glory of his Father… Truly, I say to you, there are some standing here who will not taste death until they see the kingdom of God after it has come in power."*

### Context / Setup
- Two promises close the passage: a warning (shame) and a hope (Kingdom already come)
- The Son of Man glory-scene draws on Daniel 7 — the final courtroom
- 9:1 has puzzled readers for centuries — until you look at the Greek tense
- Mark 9:2 immediately follows with the Transfiguration; 9:1 is a promise about to be kept

### Theological Anchor
- **Public confession of Christ now** ↔ **Christ's public confession of you then** (symmetry)
- The Kingdom of God is **already inaugurated in power**, not merely anticipated
- Transfiguration, Resurrection, Pentecost, Church — the Kingdom has been breaking in for 2,000 years
- The final unveiling is still future; the power is already present

### Language Notes
- **ashamed** (Greek: *epaischynomai*) — to publicly disown; in Jesus' Roman-shame culture, this meant social death
- **after it has come** (Greek: *elēlythyian*) — perfect tense participle: "already arrived and still standing." Not "about to come" but *having come, in power*
- The perfect tense is the exegetical key to 9:1

### Talking Points
- Ancient honor-shame world: to be ashamed of someone was to pretend you didn't know them
- Mark's first readers had already denied Jesus to save their skins.  Mark is not scolding — he is calling them back
- Jesus' warning is also Jesus' promise: if you confess him now, he will confess you then
- 9:1 is not a failed prediction.  The perfect tense is decisive: Jesus promises some will see the Kingdom *having already come*
- Six verses later (Mark 9:2), three of them climb a mountain.  Peter, James, John see it in full power
- The Kingdom keeps breaking in — empty tomb, Pentecost, the Church, every answered prayer, every saint dying in peace

### For Us
- The culture trains us to whisper where we should speak.  The name of Jesus sticks in our throats at holiday tables
- Jesus says: not angry, not loud — just not *ashamed*.  Your soft voice in a hallway is heard in heaven
- Many of you stand closer to the veil than anyone else in the room — you have sat by beds where the last breath was drawn
- The Kingdom is **already** here in power.  You are not waiting for proof.  You are waiting for the veil to lift
- For some in this room, "standing here who will not taste death until they see the Kingdom" is not first-century — it is a promise for this afternoon
- When you step across, you will not find you were wrong.  You will find you did not see the half of it

### Teaching Tip
9:1 is often taught as a puzzle.  Don't.  Teach it as a promise, and let the room feel how personal it is.  Many of your listeners *will* see the Kingdom soon.

### Reflection Questions

1.  Where, in your life right now, do you feel pressure to be quiet about Jesus? What would it look like to gently, not ashamed, say his name in one of those places this week?

2.  Jesus promises that the Kingdom has *already* come in power.  Where have you seen evidence of that across your lifetime — moments where God's power broke in?

3.  For many of you, the veil between this world and the next feels thin.  How does it comfort you that the Kingdom is *already* there, not just *coming*?

---

### For Reflection

This week, try two things:

- **Name one cross you are already carrying** — something about your life right now that you did not choose and cannot change.  Say it out loud to the Lord.  Pray one simple prayer: *Jesus, I am not carrying this alone.  I am carrying it behind you.*
- **Speak Jesus' name once this week** in a place where silence would be easier.  To a grandchild on the phone.  To a nurse.  To a neighbor at lunch.  You do not have to argue; you do not have to preach.  Just say his name.

Peter tried to spare Jesus the cross, and Jesus called it the work of Satan.  The world will still try to spare you the cross.  Your job is to keep walking — behind him, not ahead of him.  The road is hard.  The road is necessary.  And the road ends at a sight your eyes have been waiting your whole life to see.

*"There are some standing here who will not taste death until they see the kingdom of God after it has come in power."*

Right here.  Right now.  For whoever will follow.

### Passage Structure at a Glance

```
THE PREDICTION (v.31)
  → The Son of Man MUST suffer, be rejected, be killed, and rise
  → "Must" = divine necessity.  The cross is plan, not accident.

THE REBUKE (vv.32-33)
  → Jesus speaks plainly
  → Peter rebukes Jesus (from the front)
  → Jesus rebukes Peter (back to his proper place)
  → "Things of God" vs.  "things of men"

THE CALL TO THE CROWD (v.34)
  → Deny yourself      (decisive — once for all)
  → Take up your cross (decisive — once for all)
  → Follow me          (continuous — day after day)

THE PARADOX (vv.35-37)
  → Save your life → lose it
  → Lose your life for Jesus' sake → save it
  → The whole world cannot be traded for one soul

THE WARNING AND THE HOPE (v.38-9:1)
  → Ashamed of Jesus now → Jesus ashamed of you then
  → But: some standing here will see the Kingdom
         come in power — and they did.

ANCHOR: The Kingdom has ALREADY come.  We are not waiting for proof.
We are waiting for the veil to lift.
```
