# The Family Around Him

## A Group Study on Mark 3:31-35

> And his mother and his brothers came, and standing outside they sent to him and called him.
> And a crowd was sitting around him, and they said to him, "Your mother and your brothers are outside, seeking you."
> And he answered them, "Who are my mother and my brothers?"
> And looking about at those who sat around him, he said, "Here are my mother and my brothers!
> For whoever does the will of God, he is my brother and sister and mother."

*A note about Mark:* He wrote this Gospel for Christians in Rome who had been disowned by their families for their faith. They knew what it meant to lose a family — and to find a new one. This passage answered their deepest question: *Do I still have a family?* Jesus says yes.

---

## 1. Outside and Inside (vv.31-32)

In Jesus' world, family was everything — your identity, your safety net, your honor. As the eldest son of a widowed mother, Jesus was expected to lead the household. His departure to preach was seen as an abdication. His family came to "seize him" because they thought he had lost his mind (3:21).

Picture it: a small stone house in Capernaum, packed with people sitting on the floor. Jesus is inside, teaching. His mother and brothers stand *outside*, unable to get through. They send word in through the crowd.

The physical layout tells the spiritual story. Jesus is inside with those who chose to be there. His family is outside. The crowd begins as messengers — and ends up being called family.

### Questions

1. Have you ever felt like family was close but somehow unreachable — or far away when you needed them most?

2. Who are the people in your life right now who "show up" — who are present with you day after day?

---

## 2. The Look (vv.33-34)

"Who are my mother and my brothers?" — In a culture where questioning your own mother's identity bordered on sin, this was a theological earthquake.

Then Jesus did something only Mark records. The Greek word is *periblepsamenos* — he looked slowly, deliberately around the room. Not a glance. A sweeping gaze, taking in every face. Mark uses this word six times, always at moments of intense, personal attention.

Then: "Here are my mother and my brothers!" Not a lecture. A declaration. By saying it while looking at them, he *made* them family. These were ordinary, unimpressive people. They had not earned this. They were just... there. Sitting. Listening. Present.

And he claimed them.

### Questions

1. When in your life has someone truly *seen* you — not looked past you, but really seen you?

2. Jesus didn't say people who do God's will are *like* his family. He said "Here *are* my mother and my brothers." What's the difference?

---

## 3. Whoever (v.35)

Three things to notice:

**"Whoever"** — The Greek is deliberately open-ended. No age limit. No mobility requirement. No fine print. *Whoever.*

**"Does the will of God"** — Not the *price* of admission to God's family, but the *evidence* of belonging. What does it look like from here? Praying from a wheelchair. Speaking a kind word. Singing a hymn in the hallway. Enduring pain without bitterness. Sitting in this room, opening a Bible, and saying: *"Lord, I'm still here."*

**"Brother and sister and mother" — but not "father"** — Jesus leaves out "father" on purpose. In the new family, God alone is Father. You are not an orphan. You have a Father in heaven, and the Spirit enables you to call him *Abba* — "Daddy" — the same word Jesus used (Mark 14:36; Romans 8:15).

**About Mary:** She stood outside in Mark 3. She stood at the foot of the cross in John 19. She prayed with the disciples in Acts 1. Her journey from outside to inside took time. If someone you love is still "outside," there is hope.

### Questions

1. What does doing God's will look like for you right now, in your current situation?

2. Is there someone you're still hoping will come "inside"? What gives you patience to keep hoping?

---

### For Reflection

This week: look around your dining table, your hallway, your Bible study group. These are your brothers and sisters. Treat them that way.

Jesus looked at a room full of imperfect people and claimed them as his own. He did not check their pedigree. He looked at their willingness to sit with him — and said, *"This is my family."*

That invitation stands today. Right here. For whoever.
